\section{Predictions and Mechanism}

The theory predicts an asymmetric tradeoff. EVQ does not ``push frequencies to the low end''; instead it redistributes channel density, expanding low-frequency spacing while compressing high-frequency spacing. The anticipated gain is therefore concentrated in the regime where long-range discrimination is bottlenecked by low-frequency collisions.

This leads to a waterbed interpretation. Short-range costs should remain bounded because high-frequency channels are redundant for nearby positions, while long-range gains can be large because the low-frequency bottleneck is the dominant failure mode. The same picture predicts a base dependence: if frequencies with wavelength larger than \(L_{\mathrm{train}}\) define a collision block of relative size \(c \approx \ln L / \ln b\), then EVQ should help most when this block is large (high base, short \(L\)) and help much less when it is already tiny.

The same mechanism predicts complementarity with inference-time scaling. Geometric RoPE has two orthogonal deficiencies: a \emph{shape} deficiency (uniform log-frequency density wastes channels, limiting long-range discrimination even within $L_{\mathrm{train}}$) and a \emph{range} deficiency (frequencies designed for $L_{\mathrm{train}}$ cannot cover distances beyond it). EVQ addresses the shape deficiency by redistributing channel density within the existing frequency range, while inference-time methods such as YaRN address the range deficiency by rescaling frequencies to cover longer sequences. Figure~\ref{fig:orthogonal} makes this decomposition explicit: in log-frequency space, EVQ shifts $\phi_k$ \emph{below} the geometric diagonal (compressing high-frequency channels to free low-frequency resolution), while YaRN shifts $\phi_k$ \emph{above} it (extending the effective range beyond $L_{\mathrm{train}}$). The two corrections are additive---$\phi_{\mathrm{EVQ{+}YaRN}} = \phi_{\mathrm{EVQ}} + \Delta\phi_{\mathrm{YaRN}}$---and their orthogonality explains why the combination dramatically outperforms either intervention alone.

Finally, the theory predicts that EVQ should \emph{not} create gains when the collision-block analysis predicts little room for improvement. The low-base dead-zone experiments confirm this: a negative result in that regime is evidence that the mechanism is specific rather than overfit.

\paragraph{Empirical verification: attention distance redistribution.}
The waterbed prediction can be tested directly by comparing the attention distance distributions of trained Geometric and EVQ models. Figure~\ref{fig:attn-mechanism} (left) shows the aggregate attention density as a function of token distance, averaged across all layers, heads, and 16 validation samples on a 750M model trained at \(L{=}4096\). Two features match the theory precisely. First, a crossover emerges at approximately \(508\) tokens: below this distance, Geometric attention density exceeds EVQ, while above it the relationship reverses, with EVQ allocating up to \(2.5\times\) more attention mass at distances near \(10^3\) tokens. This is the waterbed in action---EVQ sacrifices short-range attention density (which is redundant) to gain long-range discriminability (which is the extrapolation bottleneck).

Second, a per-head analysis (Figure~\ref{fig:attn-mechanism}, right) reveals that \(295\) out of \(432\) heads (\(68\%\)) shift their average attention distance farther under EVQ, with the effect concentrated in deeper layers (L12--L17, yellow-green points). This layer-depth gradient is consistent with the expectation that deeper layers rely more on long-range contextual integration, and hence benefit most from the frequency reallocation that EVQ provides. Together, these observations confirm that EVQ's PPL and retrieval gains are not incidental metric improvements but reflect a genuine redistribution of the model's positional attention structure, as predicted by the variational theory.
