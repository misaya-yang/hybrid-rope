\subsection{Larger-scale supporting evidence}
% \label{sec:750m-supporting} % unused

The \(750\mathrm{M}\) `continue@4K' experiment is reported as supporting evidence only. The reason is not that the result is weak; on the contrary, it shows \(16\mathrm{K}\) PPL improving from \(45.1\) to \(24.4\) and \(8\mathrm{K}\) AR exact improving from \(0\%\) to \(77.5\%\). The reason is evidence hygiene: the run is single-seed, and the downstream LongBench comparison is incomplete because the remote dataset download failed during evaluation.

This distinction is important for interpretation. The larger-scale run is best viewed as evidence that the same EVQ pattern persists after continued pretraining at a longer training length, not as the main fairness anchor of the paper. The main fairness anchors remain the multi-seed \(454\mathrm{M}\) comparisons used in the main text.

\subsection{Modality-dependent \texorpdfstring{$\tau$}{tau} correction for video DiT}
\label{sec:tau-correction}

The scaling law $\tau^*=d_{\mathrm{head}}/\sqrt{L}$ matches text autoregressive models with causal attention. For bidirectional DiT, the best observed sweep point for $\tau$ is approximately $0.53\times$ the text prediction ($\tau{=}1.5$ vs.\ predicted $2.83$ for $K_t{=}16, T{=}32$). A schedule-aware decomposition writes the ratio as
\begin{equation}
\frac{\tau_{\mathrm{video}}}{\tau_{\mathrm{causal}}} \;\approx\; \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\,\sqrt{\,\mathbb{E}_t[\alpha_t^m]\,},
\label{eq:tau-video}
\end{equation}
where $1/\sqrt{2}$ is the bidirectional softmax-Jacobian factor (a diffuse bidirectional window of size $2L{+}1$ halves the directional curvature relative to a causal window of size $L$; Appendix~\ref{sec:leff-derivation}), and $\sqrt{\mathbb{E}_t[\alpha_t^m]}$ is a schedule-dependent signal-amplitude contraction with $m$ determined by the utility definition. Our training run uses a Rectified-Flow (RF) schedule $\alpha_t{=}1{-}t$ with uniform $t\!\sim\!\mathcal{U}[0,1]$ and no loss weighting (not a VP SDE). Under RF, $\mathbb{E}_t[\alpha_t]{=}0.5$, $\mathbb{E}_t[\alpha_t^2]{=}1/3$, and $\mathbb{E}_t[\alpha_t^4]{=}0.2$, giving predicted ratios $0.500$, $0.408$, and $0.316$ for $m\in\{1,2,4\}$ respectively. Only $m{=}1$ (linear signal-expectation utility) matches the empirical $0.53$ within $6\%$; the covariance-linear $m{=}2$ is $23\%$ below observation. We therefore report~\eqref{eq:tau-video} as a directional decomposition and leave a principled utility-exponent derivation under RF (specifying why $m{=}1$ rather than $m{=}2$ is the correct contraction for the RoPE phase-coherence utility) as future work.

\subsection{Cross-modal evidence: video DiT with bidirectional attention}


To test whether EVQ transfers beyond autoregressive decoders, we evaluate on a 129.6M video diffusion transformer (DiT) with bidirectional attention and 3D RoPE. We compare EVQ-Cosh ($\tau{=}1.5$) against geometric allocation ($\tau{=}0$) on Oscillating Moving MNIST \citep{srivastava2015unsupervised}, training on 32 frames and extrapolating to 128 frames ($4\times$) with temporal YaRN. Crucially, both allocations are trained \emph{within the same run} (head-to-head), sharing identical weights, optimizer state, and CUDA non-determinism---controlling for shared optimizer state and CUDA nondeterminism and isolating the implemented frequency-allocation difference within this setup.

Table~\ref{tab:dit-h2h} reports results across two independent seeds. EVQ wins every metric in both seeds. Averaging across seeds, Train MSE improves by $-26\%$, all-frame extrapolation by $-15\%$, and far-frame extrapolation by $-32\%$. The consistency across both seeds---EVQ wins all three metrics in each---suggests that the advantage is not a stochastic artifact, though we note the sample size (two seeds) is too small for formal statistical testing.

\paragraph{Dead-channel mechanism: base reduction eliminates the phase transition.}
At base${}=10{,}000$ with $K_t{=}16$ temporal frequencies and 32 training frames, the lowest-frequency channels accumulate less than $0.1$~rad of phase rotation over the entire training sequence---they are effectively dead. EVQ's $\tau$ parameter activates these channels by redistributing frequency mass, but at base${}=10{,}000$ the activation is discontinuous: $\tau{=}1.2$ leaves 11/16 channels alive (effective rank 9), while $\tau{=}1.5$ activates 12/16 (rank 10), producing the sharp phase transition observed in Table~\ref{tab:dit-h2h}.

To test this mechanism, we repeat the head-to-head experiment with base${}=1{,}000$ (same architecture, same run; 2~seeds). At this base, the geometric allocation already starts with 13/16 alive channels (rank 10), so the dead-channel bottleneck is resolved. Table~\ref{tab:dit-base1000} is consistent with the prediction: (1)~$\tau{=}1.2$ fully recovers, winning Geo by $-48\%$ far-frame MSE (vs.\ $+162\%$ at base${}=10{,}000$); (2)~$\tau{=}1.2$ and $\tau{=}1.5$ are statistically indistinguishable ($<0.3\%$ difference on all metrics), supporting the interpretation that the phase transition was driven by the dead-channel threshold rather than by an intrinsic property of $\tau{=}1.2$.

\paragraph{Dead temporal channels across video DiT architectures.}
The dead-channel problem is not specific to our experimental setup. Table~\ref{tab:dead-channels} audits five major video DiT architectures, all of which inherit base${}=10{,}000$ from text transformers. For each model, we compute the per-channel phase rotation $\theta_k \cdot T_\text{train}$ at the training temporal length, where $\theta_k = \text{base}^{-(k+0.5)/K_t}$ is the $k$-th temporal frequency. Channels with phase $<0.1$~rad are counted as dead.

Every video model wastes 32--50\% of its temporal frequency channels, because video temporal sequences (13--51 latent tokens after VAE compression) are 80--315$\times$ shorter than text sequences (${\geq}4096$ tokens) for which base${}=10{,}000$ was designed. The text reference (LLaMA-2) has zero dead channels, consistent with the dead-channel problem being specific to the sequence-length mismatch in the video temporal axis. EVQ-Cosh addresses this by redistributing frequency mass into the alive zone, and Table~\ref{tab:dit-base1000} shows that it remains effective regardless of the base choice.

Two key findings emerge in this 1--2 seed video-DiT setting. First, when all channels are alive (base${}=100$, 0/16 dead), GEO wins by ${\approx}20\%$, consistent with EVQ's advantage being specific to the dead-channel regime. Second, EVQ+YaRN is stable across base${\geq}500$ ($1.9\times$ MSE range vs.\ GEO's $12\times$), indicating robustness to the base hyperparameter within the tested grid. The best observed grid point is EVQ at base${}=10{,}000$ with YaRN ($0.0137$), better than GEO at any tested base. A pure training-length evaluation (32f, no extrapolation) shows all 12 configurations produce near-identical MSE ($<6\%$ variation), supporting the interpretation that the advantage is an extrapolation phenomenon rather than a training-distribution effect.
