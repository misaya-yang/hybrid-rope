\section{Proof Details}
\label{sec:proofs}

\subsection{Collision-only surrogate stationary density}

This subsection proves the main-text Theorem~\ref{thm:ode} (Surrogate stationary allocation) directly from the collision-only surrogate functional; the forced Fisher extension is treated separately in~\S\ref{subsec:fisher-ext}. Starting from the formal surrogate kernel
\begin{equation}
K_{\mathrm{app}}(\phi,\psi)=\alpha\delta(\phi-\psi)+\beta\min(\phi,\psi),
\end{equation}
the main-text collision functional is~\eqref{eq:Capp}, which under the mass normalization $\int_0^1\rho=1$ with Lagrange multiplier $\nu$ has constrained first variation
\begin{equation}
\alpha\rho(\phi) + \beta\,g(\phi) + \nu = 0,\qquad
g(\phi) = \int_0^1 \rho(\psi)\min(\phi,\psi)\,d\psi.
\end{equation}
\paragraph{Existence.}
For rigor, view $\mathcal{C}_{\mathrm{app}}$ first as a functional on $L^2([0,1])$ and minimize over $\mathcal{A}=\{\rho\in L^2([0,1]):\rho\ge0\ {\rm a.e.},\int_0^1\rho=1\}$. The $\alpha\|\rho\|_2^2$ term is coercive and weakly lower semicontinuous, the Green-kernel term is positive semidefinite and continuous, and the mass/nonnegativity constraints are weakly closed. A minimizer therefore exists by the direct method. Strict convexity gives uniqueness. The Euler solution derived below is strictly positive, so the nonnegativity constraint is inactive a posteriori and the classical $C^2$ derivation is justified.

Direct differentiation of $g$ gives
\begin{align}
g'(\phi)
&=
\int_\phi^1 \rho(\psi)\,d\psi,
&
g''(\phi)
&=
-\rho(\phi).
\end{align}
Differentiating the first variation twice in $\phi$ eliminates $\nu$ (a constant) and yields the homogeneous ODE
\begin{equation}
\rho''(\phi) - \tau^2\rho(\phi) = 0,\qquad \tau = \sqrt{\beta/\alpha}.
\label{eq:homogeneous-ode}
\end{equation}
Evaluating the pre-differentiated stationarity together with $g(0){=}0$, $g'(1){=}0$, and $\int_0^1\rho = 1$ gives the derivative boundary conditions
\begin{equation}
\rho'(0) = -\tau^2,\qquad \rho'(1) = 0,
\label{eq:bcs}
\end{equation}
since differentiating $\alpha\rho(\phi)+\beta g(\phi)+\nu = 0$ once and evaluating at $\phi{=}1$ gives $\alpha\rho'(1)+\beta g'(1) = 0$ hence $\rho'(1){=}0$, and at $\phi{=}0$ gives $\alpha\rho'(0)+\beta g'(0) = 0$ with $g'(0) = \int_0^1\rho = 1$, so $\rho'(0) = -\beta/\alpha = -\tau^2$. Substituting $\rho(\phi) = C_1\cosh(\tau\phi)+C_2\sinh(\tau\phi)$ into~\eqref{eq:homogeneous-ode} and~\eqref{eq:bcs} together with $\int_0^1\rho = 1$ gives the unique positive interior stationary density
\begin{equation}
\rho_\tau(\phi) \;=\; \frac{\tau\,\cosh(\tau(1-\phi))}{\sinh\tau},
\label{eq:rho-tau-closed}
\end{equation}
which is main-text Theorem~\ref{thm:ode}. The mass constraint $\int\rho{=}1$ is not lost: it enters the boundary condition at $\phi{=}0$ via $g'(0){=}\int_0^1\rho{=}1$. Positivity holds on the closed interval since $\rho_\tau(\phi){\geq}\rho_\tau(1){=}\tau/\sinh\tau{>}0$ for $\tau{>}0$ (with $\rho_\tau(0){=}\tau\coth\tau{>}0$); the inequality constraint $\rho{>}0$ is therefore inactive throughout $[0,1]$ and the unconstrained Euler--Lagrange solution coincides with the KKT-constrained solution. For the degenerate case $\beta{=}0$ the ODE reduces to $\rho''{=}0$ with $\rho'(0){=}\rho'(1){=}0$ and $\int\rho{=}1$, forcing $\rho\equiv 1$, which is also the $\tau{\to}0$ limit of~\eqref{eq:rho-tau-closed}.

\paragraph{Convexity / PSD of $\mathcal{C}_{\mathrm{app}}$.} For $\alpha{>}0$ and $\beta{\geq}0$, the surrogate is convex because both quadratic terms are positive semidefinite. The diagonal $\alpha{\int}\rho^2$ is manifestly PSD on $L^2$, and the Green-kernel cross term satisfies the identity
\begin{equation}
\iint_{[0,1]^2} f(\phi)\,f(\psi)\,\min(\phi,\psi)\,d\phi\,d\psi \;=\; \int_0^1 \!\left(\int_s^1 f(u)\,du\right)^{\!2}\!ds \;\geq\; 0.
\label{eq:min-kernel-psd}
\end{equation}
Thus the stationary density obtained above is the unique constrained \emph{minimizer} (not merely a critical point), and the surrogate is well-defined as a quadratic functional on $L^2([0,1])\supset C^2([0,1])$ with no further regularity assumption needed for the $\delta$-component. Conditional on $\mathcal{C}_{\mathrm{app}}$, this derivation is exact.

\subsection{Forced Fisher extension}
\label{subsec:fisher-ext}

Adding a Fisher utility $-\mu\!\int\rho(\phi)\,b^{-2\phi}\,d\phi$ to $\mathcal{C}_{\mathrm{app}}$ modifies the constrained stationarity to $\alpha\rho+\beta g -\mu b^{-2\phi}+\nu = 0$, whose twice-differentiated form is
\begin{equation}
\rho''(\phi) - \tau^2\rho(\phi) = \gamma\,b^{-2\phi},
\label{eq:forced-ode}
\end{equation}
with $\gamma$ set by the Fisher multiplier's sign convention. The non-resonant general solution ($\tau\neq 2\log b$) is
\begin{equation}
\rho_{\mathrm{full}}(\phi)
=
C_1\cosh(\tau\phi)+C_2\sinh(\tau\phi)+\frac{\gamma}{(2\log b)^2-\tau^2}\,b^{-2\phi}.
\end{equation}
At resonance, $\tau=2\log b$, the forcing profile $b^{-2\phi}$ coincides with one homogeneous mode. The particular solution is then
\begin{equation}
\delta\rho_F^{\mathrm{part}}(\phi)
= -\frac{\gamma}{2\tau}\,\phi e^{-\tau\phi},
\end{equation}
with the homogeneous constants again fixed by the boundary and mass conditions. All bounds below containing $(s^2-\tau^2)^{-1}$ are non-resonant bounds and should be read with $|s^2-\tau^2|^{-1}$; they are not uniform as $\tau\to 2\log b$.
EVQ-Cosh is the $\gamma{=}0$ homogeneous branch. The forced term is a controlled but nonzero residual; its magnitude and the corresponding warp-amplification caveat are bounded in~\S\ref{sec:pure-tether-bound}.

\subsection{Closed-form solution and geometric limit}

The non-resonant ODE has the closed-form solution
\begin{equation}
\rho^*(\phi)=C_1\cosh(\tau\phi)+C_2\sinh(\tau\phi)+P\,b^{-2\phi}.
\end{equation}
The first two terms span the homogeneous solution of \(\rho''-\tau^2\rho=0\), and the final term is a particular solution induced by the Fisher-weighted source \(b^{-2\phi}\). This decomposition is the source of the main paper's ``tether + pulse'' interpretation.

To translate the density into RoPE frequencies, write the cumulative map
\begin{equation}
F(\phi)=\int_0^\phi \rho^*(s)\,ds,
\end{equation}
normalize it to \([0,1]\), and invert at the discrete quantiles \(u_k=k/K\). In the pure-tether branch this inversion has the closed form
\begin{equation}
\phi_k(\tau)
=
1-\frac{1}{\tau}\operatorname{arcsinh}\!\left((1-u_k)\sinh\tau\right).
\end{equation}
To recover geometric RoPE, expand \(\sinh\tau\approx\tau\) as \(\tau\to 0\). Then
\begin{equation}
\phi_k(\tau)\to 1-\frac{1}{\tau}\operatorname{arcsinh}((1-u_k)\tau)\to u_k,
\end{equation}
which is exactly the uniform quantile map.

This also shows why the high-frequency end remains effectively anchored: for \(u_0=0\), one has \(\phi_0(\tau)=0\) exactly, so the leading inverse frequency remains \(b^0=1\), matching geometric RoPE.

\subsection{Why the pure-tether branch}

The full stationary density contains three terms: a hyperbolic tether (\(\cosh, \sinh\)) and a particular solution \(Pb^{-2\phi}\). The practical EVQ method uses only the pure-tether branch, justified on two grounds.

\textbf{(1) Closed-form invertibility.} The pure-tether density \(\rho(\phi)\propto\cosh(\tau(1-\phi))\) admits an analytically invertible CDF, yielding the closed-form warp \(\phi_k(\tau)\). Including the \(b^{-2\phi}\) term destroys this invertibility and requires numerical root-finding.

\textbf{(2) The forcing branch is globally small in $L^1$/CDF transport, not pointwise.} Once $\gamma(b) = (\lambda_F/\alpha)\,\bar\eta_F\,(2\log b)^2$ is derived (Appendix~\ref{sec:pure-tether-bound}, Eq.~\eqref{eq:gamma-explicit}), the $(s^2{-}\tau^2)^{-1}$ denominator in the particular solution $\delta\rho_F^{\mathrm{part}} = \gamma(b)\,q_s/(s^2-\tau^2)$ is cancelled by the $s^2$ in $\gamma(b)$, so the forcing amplitude is \emph{not} pointwise $O(1/\log^2 b)$. The true suppression is boundary-layer / $L^1$-level: $\|q_s\|_1 = (1-e^{-s})/s = O(1/\log b)$, giving $\|\rho_{\mathrm{full}}-\rho_{\mathrm{EVQ}}\|_1 = O(1/\log b)$ and CDF-level error of the same order (Eq.~\eqref{eq:tether-L1-correct}). The full solution improves over pure tether by $2$--$6\%$ \emph{on the offline collision/utility surrogate objective at unit-convention $\lambda{=}1$} (vs.\ $24$--$92\%$ improvement over geometric on the same objective)---this is a surrogate gap, not a trained-PPL claim, and no main empirical claim relies on the forced solution. Furthermore, since inverse-CDF inversion can amplify CDF perturbations by $1/\inf\rho_\tau{\sim}\sinh\tau/\tau$, at large $\tau$ (e.g.\ $\tau{=}4$) the forced branch is best treated as a numerical extension rather than dismissed by the $L^1$ bound; the deployed pure-tether warp captures the dominant Geo$\to$EVQ direction (24--92\%) while remaining closed-form. The 99-run Phase~16 sweep (Figure~\ref{fig:tau-sweep}) is consistent with the pure-tether family matching the scaling-law prediction, subsuming geometric RoPE as its $\tau{\to}0$ limit.

\subsection{Why constant \texorpdfstring{$\alpha$}{alpha} (and not the continuum stationary-phase coefficient)}
\label{sec:why-constant-alpha}

A natural objection asks why $\mathcal{C}_{\mathrm{app}}$ uses a constant diagonal $\alpha\,\delta(\phi{-}\psi)$ rather than the $\phi$-dependent coefficient implied by stationary-phase asymptotics of the exact kernel. With $\lambda_b{:=}\ln b$ (distinct from the transport multiplier $\lambda$ of \S\ref{sec:tau-scaling}), $D_0{:=}D(0)$, near $\psi{=}\phi$ a stationary-phase expansion of $K(\phi_1,\phi_2){=}\int D(\Delta)\cos(\omega_1\Delta)\cos(\omega_2\Delta)\,d\Delta$ gives leading diagonal coefficient $\alpha_{\mathrm{sp}}(\phi){=}\pi D_0/(L\lambda_b b^{-\phi}){=}(\pi D_0/L\lambda_b)\,b^{\phi}$, which grows exponentially in $\phi$. The difference is not a higher-order correction:
\begin{equation}
\int_0^1 \rho^2(\phi)\bigl[\alpha_K - \alpha_{\mathrm{sp}}(\phi)\bigr]d\phi
\;=\; \frac{\|\rho\|_2^2}{2K} \;-\; \frac{\pi D_0}{L\lambda_b}\!\int_0^1\!\rho^2(\phi)\,b^{\phi}\,d\phi,
\label{eq:const-vs-sp}
\end{equation}
which vanishes in the joint limit $K{\to}\infty$ \emph{and} $L\lambda_b/b{\to}\infty$, but is $O(K^{-1}{+}(L\lambda_b)^{-1}\!\cdot b)$ otherwise. Replacing $\alpha$ by $\alpha_{\mathrm{sp}}(\phi)$ converts the homogeneous Euler--Lagrange ODE $\rho''{-}\tau^2\rho{=}0$ into the variable-coefficient equation $-\bigl(\alpha_{\mathrm{sp}}(\phi)\rho\bigr)''+\beta\rho{=}0$, whose closed-form solution is a modified-Bessel combination $\rho(\phi){\propto}e^{-\lambda_b\phi}[A\,I_0(x(\phi))+B\,K_0(x(\phi))]$ with $x(\phi){=}(2r/\lambda_b)e^{-\lambda_b\phi/2}$, $r{=}\sqrt{\beta L\lambda_b/(\pi D_0)}$. This Bessel density has heavier right tail than $\cosh$ and, in some parameter regimes ($x_0{\to}\infty,x_1{\to}0$), violates positivity at $\phi{=}1$ unless an active inequality constraint is added. We deliberately use constant $\alpha$ for three reasons. (i)~\emph{Closed-form CDF}: the cosh density admits the elementary inverse-CDF $\phi_k(\tau){=}1-\tau^{-1}\mathrm{arsinh}((1{-}u_k)\sinh\tau)$, which is the prerequisite for inverse-CDF channel quantization; the Bessel density does not. (ii)~\emph{Manifest positivity}: $\rho_\tau{\geq}\tau/\sinh\tau{>}0$ on $[0,1]$ for all $\tau{>}0$, while the variable-$\alpha$ Bessel solution can violate $\rho{>}0$ at the right endpoint. (iii)~\emph{Discrete-grid fit, not continuum asymptotic}: paper-deployed $\alpha{\approx}1/d_{\mathrm{rot}}$ is fitted to $K$ on the discrete $K{\in}\{16,32\}$ grid (\S\ref{sec:tau-scaling}), and the resulting cosh allocation is functionally validated against the exact kernel by the 24--92\% collision reduction across 12 configurations (\S\ref{sec:surrogate-validation}). $\mathcal{C}_{\mathrm{app}}$ is therefore a deliberately discrete surrogate, not a claim about continuum-limit asymptotics; \eqref{eq:const-vs-sp} is the precise statement of how the two diverge.

\subsection{Exact higher-order displacement floor}
\label{sec:floor-higher-order}

The one-channel mid-displacement threshold admits an exact Taylor series in $1/K$ that is fully closed-form (no empirical calibration).

\begin{proposition}[Higher-order floor]
\label{prop:floor-higher-order}
For the EVQ-Cosh mapping with $K = d_{\mathrm{rot}}/2$ channels, the $N$-channel mid-displacement threshold satisfies
\begin{equation}
\tau_{\mathrm{floor}}(N, K) \;=\; 4\sqrt{\frac{N}{K}}\left[1 + \frac{N}{2K} + \frac{241}{120}\left(\frac{N}{K}\right)^{\!2} + O(K^{-3})\right].
\end{equation}
All coefficients are derived from the Taylor series
\(\Delta\varphi(1/2, \tau) = -\tau^2/16 + \tau^4/256 + 17\tau^6/30720 + O(\tau^8)\),
whose coefficients $(-1/16,\, +1/256,\, +17/30720)$ are exact rationals from the expansion of $\operatorname{arcsinh}(\sinh\tau/2)/\tau$ (Appendix script \texttt{theory\_B\_floor\_higher\_order.py}; verified symbolically).
\end{proposition}

\paragraph{Accuracy.} Against the exact floor (obtained by numerically solving $|\Delta\varphi(1/2, \tau)| = N/K$): the leading term $4\sqrt{N/K}$ has relative error $3.85\%$ at $K{=}16$ and $0.20\%$ at $K{=}512$; the two-term expansion reduces this to $0.088\%$ at $K{=}16$ and below $10^{-5}$ for $K{\geq}128$. The formula is thus a closed-form refinement of the Habitable Zone lower bound, with all constants derived from the cosh geometry alone.

\subsection{Waterbed envelope and corollary}
\label{sec:waterbed-proof}

\paragraph{Setup.} With $\rho{>}0$, $\int_0^1\rho{=}1$, $U(d\phi){=}d\phi$, $P_\rho(d\phi){=}\rho\,d\phi$, $\delta\rho{=}\rho{-}1$, and $M_\rho(s){=}\int_s^1\delta\rho\,du$, define the waterbed functional
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{W}(\rho) \;\coloneqq\; \alpha\,\chi^2(U\|P_\rho) + \beta\int_0^1 M_\rho(s)^2\,ds,\qquad \chi^2(U\|P_\rho) = \int_0^1 \frac{(1-\rho)^2}{\rho}\,d\phi.
\label{eq:waterbed-functional}
\end{equation}
The first term is the Pearson divergence of $U$ from $P_\rho$ (equivalently, $\int 1/\rho - 1$); the second is the integrated squared cumulative error, which arises from the $\min$-kernel off-diagonal covariance in $K_{\mathrm{app}}$.

\begin{proposition}[Waterbed inequality]
\label{prop:waterbed}
Let $D_B(\rho) = -\int_0^1\log\rho(\phi)\,d\phi$ be the Burg entropy deficit. Then
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{W}(\rho) \;\ge\; \alpha\,F_{*}(D_B(\rho)),\qquad F_{*}(d) \;=\; \inf_{\rho>0,\,\int\rho=1,\,-\int\log\rho=d}\left[\int_0^1\frac{1}{\rho}-1\right],
\label{eq:waterbed-tight}
\end{equation}
with equality $\mathcal{W}(\rho) = 0$ if and only if $\rho \equiv 1$ (geometric RoPE). A readable corollary via R\'enyi monotonicity is
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{W}(\rho) \;\ge\; \alpha\bigl(e^{D_B(\rho)} - 1\bigr).
\label{eq:waterbed-corollary}
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
Since $\beta\int M_\rho^2\ge 0$, $\mathcal{W}(\rho)\ge\alpha\chi^2(U\|P_\rho)$. For fixed $D_B(\rho){=}d$, the sharp lower envelope of $\chi^2$ over all densities is the Csisz\'ar--Vajda $f$-divergence comparison $F_{*}$, whose extremizer is a two-level density $X=1/\rho\in\{a,c\}$ with mass-normalization and entropy-deficit constraints. For the corollary, $1+\chi^2(U\|P_\rho) = \int 1/\rho\,\mathbb{E}_{U}[\cdot] \ge e^{D_1(U\|P_\rho)} = e^{D_B(\rho)}$ by R\'enyi-2 $\ge$ R\'enyi-1. Equality at $\rho{\equiv}1$ is immediate from both sides vanishing.
\end{proof}

\paragraph{EVQ evaluation.} For $\rho_\tau(\phi) = \tau\cosh(\tau(1{-}\phi))/\sinh\tau$ (Eq.~\eqref{eq:S-chi2-evq} below uses the un-normalized integral; cf.\ \S\ref{sec:chi2-load} ``Normalization convention''),
\begin{align}
D_B(\rho_\tau) \;&=\; \log\tfrac{\sinh\tau}{\tau} - \tfrac{1}{\tau}\int_0^\tau\log\cosh y\,dy \;=\; \tfrac{\tau^4}{90} - \tfrac{8\tau^6}{2835} + O(\tau^8),\label{eq:D-B-evq}\\
S_{\chi^2}(\rho_\tau) \;&=\; \tfrac{\sinh\tau}{\tau^2}\arctan(\sinh\tau) - 1 \;=\; \tfrac{\tau^4}{45} - \tfrac{2\tau^6}{315} + O(\tau^8),\label{eq:S-chi2-evq}\\
\int_0^1 M_{\rho_\tau}(s)^2\,ds \;&=\; \tfrac{2\tau^4}{945} + O(\tau^6).\label{eq:M-rho-evq}
\end{align}
Combining,
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{W}(\rho_\tau) \;=\; \left(\frac{\alpha}{45}+\frac{2\beta}{945}\right)\tau^4 + O(\tau^6),\qquad \mathcal{W}(\rho_{\tau_*}) \;=\; O\!\left(\frac{d_{\mathrm{head}}^4}{(L_{\mathrm{eff}}^{J})^2}\right).
\label{eq:waterbed-evq-scale}
\end{equation}
The in-range surrogate cost for EVQ is $O(\tau^4)$; since $\tau_*^2 \asymp d_{\mathrm{head}}^2/L_{\mathrm{eff}}^{J}$, the cost scales as $d_{\mathrm{head}}^4/(L_{\mathrm{eff}}^{J})^2$. This is the explicit form of ``bounded in-range cost'' cited in the main text.

\subsection{Surrogate self-consistency theorem}
\label{sec:self-consistency}

The surrogate functional $\mathcal{J}[\rho]$ admits three exact identities that connect its two integral terms $T_1(\tau) = \int_0^1 \rho^2\,d\phi$ and $T_2(\tau) = \int_0^1\!\!\int_0^1 \rho(\phi)\rho(\psi)\min(\phi,\psi)\,d\phi\,d\psi$, where $\rho(\phi) = \tau\cosh(\tau(1{-}\phi))/\sinh\tau$ is the normalized cosh density.

\begin{theorem}[Surrogate self-consistency]
\label{thm:self-consistency}
For all $\tau > 0$:
\begin{equation}
\tau^2 T_2(\tau) + T_1(\tau) = \tau\coth\tau.
\label{eq:self-consistency}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The Green's function $g(\phi) = \int_0^1 \rho(\psi)\min(\phi,\psi)\,d\psi$ satisfies $g''(\phi) = -\rho(\phi)$ with $g(0) = 0$, $g'(1) = 0$ (from the $\min$ kernel structure; see~\S\ref{sec:proofs}). Define $h(\phi) = \rho(0) - \rho(\phi)$. Then $h'' = -\rho''$, and under the pure-tether ODE $\rho'' = \tau^2\rho$ this gives $h'' = -\tau^2\rho$, with $h(0) = 0$ and $h'(0) = -\rho'(0)$.

Define $f(\phi) = \tau^2 g(\phi) - h(\phi)$. Then $f'' = \tau^2 g'' - h'' = -\tau^2\rho - (-\tau^2\rho) = 0$, so $f$ is linear in $\phi$. The boundary values give $f(0) = \tau^2 g(0) - h(0) = 0$ and $f'(0) = \tau^2 g'(0) + \rho'(0) = \tau^2 - \tau^2 = 0$ (using $g'(0) = \int_0^1\rho = 1$ and $\rho'(0) = -\tau^2$ from the pure-tether density). Hence $f \equiv 0$, yielding $\tau^2 g(\phi) = h(\phi) = \rho(0) - \rho(\phi)$, i.e.,
\begin{equation}
g(\phi) = \frac{\rho(0) - \rho(\phi)}{\tau^2}.
\label{eq:green-identity}
\end{equation}
Integrating against $\rho$:
\begin{equation}
T_2 = \int_0^1 \rho(\phi)\,g(\phi)\,d\phi = \frac{1}{\tau^2}\bigl[\rho(0)\underbrace{\int_0^1\!\rho}_{=1} - T_1\bigr]
= \frac{\rho(0) - T_1}{\tau^2}.
\end{equation}
Since $\rho(0) = \tau\cosh\tau/\sinh\tau = \tau\coth\tau$, rearranging gives~\eqref{eq:self-consistency}.
\end{proof}

An immediate corollary follows.

\begin{corollary}[Closed-form $T_2$]
\begin{equation}
T_2(\tau) = \frac{\sinh 2\tau - 2\tau}{4\tau\sinh^2\tau}.
% \label{eq:T2-closed} % unused
\end{equation}
\end{corollary}

This follows from substituting the known closed form $T_1(\tau) = \tau(1 + \sinh 2\tau / 2\tau) / (2\sinh^2\tau)$ and $\rho(0) = \tau\coth\tau$ into the theorem. Both identities are verified numerically to machine precision ($<10^{-15}$ relative error across $\tau \in [0.01, 20]$; code: \texttt{scripts/lambda\_identity.py}).

The self-consistency theorem reveals that the surrogate's two energy terms are not independent: they are locked by the cosh density's self-reproducing property under the $\min$-kernel Green's function. This structural rigidity is why the pure-tether solution (\S\ref{sec:proofs}) performs so well despite being a single-parameter family.

\subsection{Cross-validation of \texorpdfstring{$\lambda$}{lambda}}
\label{sec:lambda-cv}

The operating-point rule $\tau^* = c\cdot d_{\mathrm{head}}/\sqrt{L}$ is analyzed at two levels.

\paragraph{Collision-score level.} We minimize the exact-kernel collision score $C(\tau)$ for each of 9 configurations ($d_{\mathrm{head}} \in \{32, 64, 128\}$, $L \in \{256, 512, 1024\}$) and compute $c_{\mathrm{coll}} = \tau^*_{\mathrm{coll}} / (d_{\mathrm{head}}/\sqrt{L})$. The resulting $c_{\mathrm{coll}} \approx 1.17$ is nearly constant (CV: $0.11\%$); leave-one-out cross-validation gives max prediction error $<0.4\%$ (Table~\ref{tab:lambda-cv}). This near-zero spread reflects the smoothness of the collision score as a function of $\tau$, not PPL variability.

\input{tables/table_lambda_cv}

\paragraph{PPL-sweep level.} Phase~16 training sweeps (9 configurations $\times$ 3+ seeds) identify the best observed PPL sweep point for each configuration. The resulting $\lambda_{\mathrm{PPL}} = \tau^*_{\mathrm{PPL}} / (d_{\mathrm{head}}/\sqrt{L})$ has mean ${\approx}1.19$ with broader scatter (CV ${\approx}14\%$), because PPL depends on training noise, finite data, and the discrete sweep grid. The deployed formula uses $\lambda{=}1$ (i.e.\ $\tau = d_{\mathrm{head}}/\sqrt{L}$ without a multiplicative constant), which consistently falls inside the flat PPL basin: the PPL gap between the formula and the best observed grid point is ${<}1\%$ across all 27 tested configurations.

\paragraph{Basin flatness.} The collision-score landscape $C(\tau)$ for a representative configuration ($d_{\mathrm{head}}{=}64$, $L{=}512$, $b{=}500\mathrm{K}$) is flat near the optimum: ${\pm}20\%$ deviation from $\tau^*_{\mathrm{coll}}$ incurs ${<}1\%$ collision increase. This flatness is why the deployed bare formula $\tau = d_{\mathrm{head}}/\sqrt{L}$ (i.e.\ omitting the $\sqrt{45\,Q_1(L,b)}$ prefactor for practical simplicity, so $c_{\mathrm{deployed}}{=}1$) sits ${\approx}15\%$ below $c_{\mathrm{coll}}{=}1.171$---inside the ${\pm}20\%$ collision basin---and consequently inside the ${<}1\%$ PPL basin observed in trained sweeps. The full theoretical prediction $c_{\mathrm{pred}}{=}\sqrt{45\,Q_1}{\approx}1.19$ matches $c_{\mathrm{coll}}$ to $2\%$ (see ``Numerical validation'' below); the practical and theoretical prefactors differ, but both land inside the same flat basin.

\paragraph{$b$-dependence of $c_{\mathrm{pred}}$.} The basin width above is verified at the representative $(d_{\mathrm{head}},L,b){=}(64,512,500\mathrm{K})$. The theoretical prefactor $c_{\mathrm{pred}}(L,b){=}\sqrt{45\,Q_1(L,b)}$ varies modestly with $b$: at $b{=}500\mathrm{K}$ across $L{\in}[128,4096]$, $c_{\mathrm{pred}}{\in}[1.14,1.20]$ and the deployed $c{=}1$ deviates 12--17\% (inside the ${\pm}20\%$ collision basin); at $b{=}100\mathrm{K}$ the range is $[1.06,1.20]$ and deployed $c{=}1$ stays inside the basin throughout; at $b{=}10\mathrm{K}$, $c_{\mathrm{pred}}$ drops to $0.94$ at $L{=}2048$ ($+7\%$ deviation, still inside the basin) and $0.80$ at $L{=}4096$ ($+25\%$ deviation, marginally \emph{outside} the ${\pm}20\%$ basin). All primary experiments use $b{\geq}500\mathrm{K}$ where the bare formula stays inside the basin; at small $b$ and large $L$, reverting to the explicit $c_{\mathrm{pred}}(L,b){=}\sqrt{45\,Q_1(L,b)}$ prefactor is appropriate.

\paragraph{Why $\lambda$ is a normalization convention, not a free parameter.} The Lagrange multiplier $\lambda$ in the softmax-transport functional $\mathcal{F}(\tau) = \tfrac{1}{2}S_{\chi^2}(\tau) - \lambda\,U(\tau,L)$ has a closed-form interpretation. We use $d_{\mathrm{head}}$ (not the number of RoPE frequency pairs $K = d_{\mathrm{head}}/2$) as the stiffness normalization throughout both main text and appendix; all factors of two arising from the RoPE pair structure are absorbed into the $\mathcal{O}(1)$ convention-dependent prefactor. We adopt the Dirichlet-energy convention in which $S_{\chi^2}$ enters $\mathcal{F}$ with a $\tfrac{1}{2}$ prefactor, so that under the small-$\tau$ expansion $\tfrac{1}{2}S_{\chi^2}(\tau) = \tau^4/(90\,d_{\mathrm{head}}) + O(\tau^6)$ (using $S_{\chi^2}(\tau) = \tau^4/(45\,d_{\mathrm{head}})$ from \S\ref{sec:stiffness-derivation}); this matches the numeric constant $45$ appearing in Proposition~\ref{prop:softmax-transport}. Similarly, $U(\tau,L) = (d_{\mathrm{head}}/L)[Q_0 + \tau^2 Q_1(L,b)] + O(\tau^4)$, where $Q_1(L,b) = \int_0^1 \eta(\phi)\,q(Lb^{-\phi})\,d\phi$ is an explicit integral of known functions. All numerical factors below are reported under this $\tfrac{1}{2}S$ convention; a reader using an un-normalized $S$ would absorb a factor of $2$ into $\lambda$. The balance equation $\partial\mathcal{F}/\partial\tau = 0$ yields
\[
\tau_*^2 \;=\; 45\,\lambda\,Q_1(L,b)\cdot\frac{d_{\mathrm{head}}^2}{L}.
\]
Splitting $Q_1$ into an active-channel contribution ($q \approx 1/2$ for $Lb^{-\phi} \gg 1$, i.e.\ $\phi \in [0, \phi^*]$ with $\phi^* = \ln L/\ln b$) and a dead-channel contribution ($q(x) = x^4/45 + O(x^6)$ for $x = Lb^{-\phi} \ll 1$ on $\phi \in [\phi^*, 1]$; the leading Taylor coefficient is $x^4/45$, not $x^2/45$) gives the closed form $Q_1(L,b) \approx \tfrac{1}{12}\bigl[1 - (1-\phi^*)^3 - \phi^*\bigr]$ to leading order, with the dead-channel correction exponentially small. To avoid symbol reuse, we reserve $\lambda$ for the utility-weight normalization in $\mathcal{F}(\tau){=}\tfrac{1}{2}S_{\chi^2}(\tau){-}\lambda U(\tau,L)$ and write the multiplicative operating-point prefactor as $c$, so $\tau^{*} = c\cdot d_{\mathrm{head}}/\sqrt{L}$. Combining the expansions above gives the theoretical prefactor $c_{\mathrm{pred}}(L,b;\lambda) = \sqrt{45\,\lambda\,Q_1(L,b)}$. Under the deployed convention $\lambda{=}1$, this is a posterior-consistency prediction (computable from $(L,b)$), not a per-experiment fitted hyperparameter; the residual $\mathcal{O}(1)$ offset is absorbed into the flat PPL basin rather than treated as a tunable parameter.

\paragraph{Probabilistic interpretation of $q(x)$.} The function $q(x){=}1/2{+}\sin(2x)/(4x){-}(\sin x/x)^2$ is not a postulated form; it is the cosine phase-variance under a uniform separation prior $t \sim \mathrm{Unif}[0,1]$:
\[
q(x) \;=\; \mathrm{Var}_{t \sim U[0,1]}\!\bigl[\cos(xt)\bigr]
       \;=\; \mathbb{E}_t\!\bigl[\cos^2(xt)\bigr] - \bigl(\mathbb{E}_t\!\bigl[\cos(xt)\bigr]\bigr)^2,
\]
since $\mathbb{E}_t[\cos^2(xt)] = 1/2 + \sin(2x)/(4x)$ and $\mathbb{E}_t[\cos(xt)] = \sin(x)/x$. Operationally, $q(Lb^{-\phi})$ measures how much information channel $\phi$ retains about a uniformly distributed relative position over $[0,L]$: in the dead-channel tail ($Lb^{-\phi}\ll 1$) the cosine is nearly constant and $q \to 0$; in the active region ($Lb^{-\phi}\gg 1$) the phase is well-sampled and $q \to 1/2$. Thus $Q_1(L,b)$ weights the cosh-driven density perturbation $\eta(\phi)$ by the per-channel phase-variance budget, and $q(x)$ is a self-contained signal-processing quantity rather than an unmotivated kernel.

\subsection{Scaling analysis of \texorpdfstring{$\tau^*$}{tau*}: from surrogate to operating point}
\label{sec:tau-scaling}

The operating-point rule
\begin{equation}
\tau^* = \frac{d_{\mathrm{head}}}{\sqrt{L}}
\end{equation}
is supported across 99 trained models spanning 27 validation settings (Figure~\ref{fig:tau-sweep}); systems and MLA claims rely on 3-seed anchors within this grid, while the PE-dominant diagnostic and 1--2-seed rows extend scope. Here we analyze to what extent the broadband surrogate predicts this formula, and identify the gap that remains.

\paragraph{Numerical characterization of surrogate coefficients.}
We fit $K_{\mathrm{app}}(\phi_i,\phi_j) = \alpha\delta_{ij}/\Delta\phi + \beta\min(\phi_i,\phi_j)$ to the exact collision kernel $K(\phi_i,\phi_j) = \frac{1}{2L}\bigl[\frac{\sin((\omega_1{-}\omega_2)L)}{\omega_1{-}\omega_2} + \frac{\sin((\omega_1{+}\omega_2)L)}{\omega_1{+}\omega_2}\bigr]$ evaluated at $K = d_{\mathrm{head}}/2$ uniformly spaced channel positions in $[0,1]$. Sweeping across $L \in \{128,\ldots,4096\}$, $d_{\mathrm{head}} \in \{32,64,128,256\}$, and $b \in \{10\mathrm{K}, 100\mathrm{K}, 500\mathrm{K}\}$ yields:
\begin{equation}
\alpha \;\approx\; \frac{1}{2K} \;=\; \frac{1}{d_{\mathrm{head}}},
\qquad
\beta \;\sim\; L^{-0.22},\; O(1).
\end{equation}
The diagonal coefficient $\alpha$ is set by the kernel's diagonal value ($K(\phi,\phi) \approx 1/2$ for most channels) times the channel spacing $\Delta\phi = 1/K$. Crucially, $\alpha$ depends on $d_{\mathrm{head}}$ through the discretization ($K = d_{\mathrm{head}}/2$) and is nearly independent of $L$. The off-diagonal coefficient $\beta$ has weak power-law decay with $L$ and negligible $d_{\mathrm{head}}$ dependence.

\paragraph{What the surrogate predicts.}
Substituting into $\tau_{\mathrm{surr}} = \sqrt{\beta/\alpha}$:
\begin{equation}
\tau_{\mathrm{surr}} = \sqrt{\beta \cdot d_{\mathrm{head}}} \;\sim\; \sqrt{d_{\mathrm{head}}} \cdot L^{-0.11}.
\end{equation}
This correctly captures the $d_{\mathrm{head}}$ dependence: $\alpha \propto 1/d_{\mathrm{head}}$ implies $\tau \propto \sqrt{d_{\mathrm{head}}}$, matching the direction and monotonicity of the observed scaling. However, the $L$-scaling is too weak: $L^{-0.11}$ vs.\ the observed $L^{-0.5}$.

\paragraph{What the surrogate does and does not supply.}
Under the probability-density convention used throughout the paper, $\int_0^1\rho=1$; the number of rotary channels enters through the deployed discrete grid and through the fitted diagonal coefficient $\alpha\approx 1/d_{\mathrm{rot}}$ (for standard MHA, $d_{\mathrm{rot}}=d_{\mathrm{head}}$). Thus the broadband surrogate supplies the cosh allocation family and the finite-channel normalization direction, but it does not by itself derive the deployed operating rule $\tau=d_{\mathrm{eff}}/\sqrt{L}$.

The remaining $L^{-1/2}$ structure comes from the separate post-softmax transport calculation in Proposition~\ref{prop:softmax-transport}: the diffuse softmax Jacobian contributes the $1/L$ curvature factor, and balancing the resulting $O(\tau^2)$ utility against the Pearson-$\chi^2$ $O(\tau^4)$ stiffness gives the structural scaling
\begin{equation}
\tau_*^2 \propto \frac{d_{\mathrm{eff}}^2}{L}.
\end{equation}
The practical rule $\tau=d_{\mathrm{eff}}/\sqrt{L}$ is therefore a semi-analytic operating-point selector with a convention-dependent $\mathcal{O}(1)$ prefactor, validated inside the observed flat basin, not a global optimum of either the full trained-transformer objective or the exact oscillatory kernel.

\paragraph{Why $\chi^2$.} Two independent arguments favor $p{=}1$. (1)~\emph{Attention-load asymmetry}: each frequency channel at density $\rho(\phi)$ bears per-channel attention load ${\propto}1/\rho$; weighting the squared departure by this load yields $\int(\rho{-}1)^2/\rho = \chi^2$. (2)~\emph{Discrete-grid effect}: the continuous $L^2$ integral, evaluated on EVQ's non-uniform cosh-quantile grid, naturally acquires a $1/\rho$ Jacobian factor, producing discrete $\chi^2$.

\paragraph{Closed form.} For $p{=}1$, the stiffness admits
\begin{equation}
S_{\chi^2}(\tau) = \frac{1}{d_{\mathrm{head}}}\!\left[\frac{\sinh\tau\cdot\arctan(\sinh\tau)}{\tau^2} - 1\right],
\end{equation}
verified numerically to relative error ${<}7{\times}10^{-6}$.

\paragraph{Exponent-matched diagnostic.} As a sensitivity analysis, one can ask which member of the $S_p$ family would match the target exponent $\tau_* \propto 1/\sqrt{L}$ exactly over this grid. The resulting $S_{\mathrm{derived}}(\tau)$ lies near $p \approx 0.85$ (high-resolution sweep; consistent with $0.80$ from the coarser grid), close to but distinct from the Pearson $\chi^2$ choice $p{=}1$. We do not use this as a derivation of the stiffness; it is reported as sensitivity analysis. The full sweep is reproduced by \texttt{scripts/analysis/verify\_stiffness\_and\_regime.py} (pure NumPy, ${\sim}60$s).

\subsection{Training-regime dependence and the LoRA phase transition}
\label{sec:lora-regime}

Pretrained weights create a \emph{coupling stiffness} that penalizes frequency redistribution. This subsection gives a phenomenological model calibrated by the rank sweep, not a first-principles derivation: the EVQ warp actually perturbs frequencies at order $\tau^2$ (Eq.~\eqref{eq:quantile-displacement}), so the $\tau^2$ coupling below is an \emph{effective} single-scale surrogate for the leading non-perturbative interaction with frozen pretrained $Q/K$ blocks, not the literal leading term of the small-$\tau$ expansion. Treating the frozen-weight mismatch as producing an aggregate logit variance $\mathrm{Var}(\delta\ell) \propto \tau^2$, the total stiffness becomes
\begin{equation}
S_{\mathrm{total}}(\tau; r) = S_{\chi^2}(\tau) + \Lambda_0\!\left(1 - \frac{r}{K}\right)\frac{\tau^2}{d_{\mathrm{head}}},
\end{equation}
where $r$ is the LoRA rank, $K = d_{\mathrm{head}}/2$, and $\Lambda_0 \gg 1$ for well-trained models. In this phenomenological model the coupling term dominates at small~$\tau$, and the system exhibits a \emph{near-phase-transition} in effective viability: $\tau_* \to 0$ for small $r/K$ and $\tau_*$ jumps to an $\mathcal{O}(1)$ value once $r$ is large enough.

Numerical verification under this phenomenological model shows the transition is \emph{apparent-sharp}: for LLaMA-8B parameters ($d_{\mathrm{head}}{=}128$, $K{=}64$), sweeping LoRA rank over the variational landscape shows $\tau_*{=}0$ for all $r \leq 48$ ($r/K \leq 0.75$), then $\tau_*$ jumps to $1.41$ at $r{=}64$ ($r/K{=}1.0$). The rank at which this occurs, $r \approx K$, is the value observed in this sweep; we do not claim an exact first-principles derivation of $r_c = K$, and the specific threshold depends on $\Lambda_0$.

For LLaMA-8B with LoRA $r{=}16$: the adaptation ratio $r/K = 25\%$ leaves 48 of 64 frequency channels frozen. At $\tau{=}1.414$, approximately 8 channels are displaced, but the 48 frozen channels cannot adapt, creating systematic attention logit mismatch and driving PPL from 11.8 to~77.1 (this PPL value is reproduced after calibrating $\Lambda_0$ on the same observation, so it is a calibration check of the phenomenological model rather than an out-of-sample prediction). The qualitative implication---LoRA with $r \gtrsim K$ should restore EVQ viability---is independent of this calibration and is consistent with the rank sweep in Table~\ref{tab:lora-8b}.

\paragraph{SFT coupling decay.} Under full-parameter SFT, the coupling decays exponentially: $\tau^*_{\mathrm{SFT}}(T) \approx \tau^*_{\mathrm{pt}} / \sqrt{1 + \Lambda_0 e^{-T\eta\sigma}}$. Recovery to $70\%$ of $\tau^*_{\mathrm{pt}}$ requires $T\eta\sigma \approx 5$; full recovery ($>95\%$) requires $T\eta\sigma \approx 10$.

\subsection{Effective window \texorpdfstring{$L_{\mathrm{eff}}^{J}$}{L\_eff\^J} via softmax-Jacobian Rayleigh quotient}
\label{sec:leff-derivation}

\paragraph{From density perturbation to RoPE frequency shift.}
The EVQ pure-tether CDF is $F_\tau(\phi) = 1 - \sinh(\tau(1{-}\phi))/\sinh\tau$, with inverse-CDF warp $\Phi_\tau(u) = 1 - \tau^{-1}\operatorname{arsinh}((1{-}u)\sinh\tau)$. Setting $\theta{=}\tau^2$ and expanding $\rho_\tau(\phi) = 1 + \theta\, a(\phi) + O(\theta^2)$ with $a(\phi) = (1{-}\phi)^2/2 - 1/6$, the quantile displacement takes the closed form
\begin{equation}
\Phi_\tau(u) \;=\; u + \tau^2\, h(u) + O(\tau^4), \qquad h(u) \;=\; -\!\int_0^u a(v)\,dv \;=\; -\tfrac{1}{6}u(1{-}u)(2{-}u),
\label{eq:quantile-displacement}
\end{equation}
so RoPE frequencies perturb as $\omega_k(\tau) = \omega_k^{(0)}[1 - \tau^2(\log b)\,h(u_k) + O(\tau^4)]$ with $\omega_k^{(0)}{=}b^{-u_k}$. Hence $\dot\omega_k \coloneqq \partial_{\theta}\omega_k|_{\tau=0} = -(\log b)\,\omega_k^{(0)} h(u_k)$.

\paragraph{Explicit RoPE logit transport direction.}
Fix a layer/head and query $i$; write each $2$D RoPE pair as $q_{i,k}^{\mathbb{C}} = q_{i,2k{-}1}{+}iq_{i,2k}$, $k_{j,k}^{\mathbb{C}} = k_{j,2k{-}1}{+}ik_{j,2k}$, and $\alpha_{ij,k} = q_{i,k}^{\mathbb{C}}\overline{k_{j,k}^{\mathbb{C}}} = C_{ij,k} + iD_{ij,k}$. With relative position $r_{ij}$ and attention scale $s_{\mathrm{att}}$, the RoPE logit is $z_{ij}^{\mathrm{rope}}(\tau) = s_{\mathrm{att}}^{-1}\operatorname{Re}\sum_{k}\alpha_{ij,k}\exp(i r_{ij}\omega_k(\tau))$. The leading logit transport direction $g_i(j) \coloneqq \partial_\theta z_{ij}^{\mathrm{rope}}|_{\tau=0}$ has the closed form
\begin{equation}
g_i(j) \;=\; \frac{(\log b)\,r_{ij}}{s_{\mathrm{att}}}\sum_{k=1}^{K} \omega_k^{(0)}\,h(u_k)\!\left[\,C_{ij,k}\sin(r_{ij}\omega_k^{(0)}) + D_{ij,k}\cos(r_{ij}\omega_k^{(0)})\,\right].
\label{eq:g-closed-form}
\end{equation}
This is the Fr\'echet derivative of the logit with respect to the EVQ allocation perturbation at fixed activations. A finite-$\tau$ implementation that avoids first-order phase expansion at large $r_{ij}\omega$ is
\begin{equation}
g_i^{(\tau)}(j) \;=\; \frac{1}{s_{\mathrm{att}}\tau^2}\operatorname{Re}\sum_{k=1}^{K}\alpha_{ij,k}\!\left[\exp(ir_{ij}b^{-\Phi_\tau(u_k)}) - \exp(ir_{ij}b^{-u_k})\right],
\label{eq:g-finite-tau}
\end{equation}
with $g_i(j) = \lim_{\tau\to 0} g_i^{(\tau)}(j)$.

\paragraph{Primary definition: Rayleigh quotient of the softmax Jacobian.}
For the trained attention distribution $p_i(j)$ on keys $j\!\in\!\mathcal{K}_i$, $J_{\mathrm{sm}}(p_i) = \mathrm{diag}(p_i) - p_ip_i^\top$ satisfies
$g_i^\top J_{\mathrm{sm}}(p_i) g_i = \mathrm{Var}_{j\sim p_i}[g_i(j)] = \tfrac{1}{2}\sum_{j,k} p_i(j)p_i(k)(g_i(j)-g_i(k))^2.$
Since $J_{\mathrm{sm}}(p_i)\mathbf{1}{=}0$, we center $g_i$ by the key-wise mean operator $P_i$. The effective length is then defined by the \emph{ratio-of-means}
\begin{equation}
\frac{1}{L_{\mathrm{eff}}^{J}} \;=\; \kappa_{\mathrm{att}} \;=\; \frac{\mathbb{E}_{\ell,h,i}\!\left[\,g_i^\top J_{\mathrm{sm}}(p_i)g_i\,\right]}{\mathbb{E}_{\ell,h,i}\!\left[\,\|P_i g_i\|_2^2\,\right]},
\label{eq:leff-ratio-of-means}
\end{equation}
which is the directional post-softmax curvature in the RoPE transport direction, averaged with the natural utility weight $\|P_ig_i\|_2^2$. We prefer~\eqref{eq:leff-ratio-of-means} over an average of per-query ratios because it avoids noise amplification when $\|P_ig_i\|_2$ is small. Substituting $1/L$ by $1/L_{\mathrm{eff}}^{J}$ in Proposition~\ref{prop:softmax-transport} gives
\begin{align}
\tau_*^2 \;&=\; 45\,\lambda\,Q_1^{\mathrm{eff}}(L,b)\,\frac{d_{\mathrm{head}}^2}{L_{\mathrm{eff}}^{J}}, \qquad \tau^{*}\approx \frac{d_{\mathrm{head}}}{\sqrt{L_{\mathrm{eff}}^{J}}}, \notag\\
\frac{d\log\tau^{*}}{d\log L_{\mathrm{train}}}\;&=\;-\tfrac{1}{2}\,a(L_{\mathrm{train}}), \qquad a\;\coloneqq\;\frac{d\log L_{\mathrm{eff}}^{J}}{d\log L_{\mathrm{train}}}.
\label{eq:tau-leff}
\end{align}
The paper's $-1/2$ exponent is the case $a{=}1$ (diffuse growth); $a{\to}0$ corresponds to saturation $L_{\mathrm{eff}}^{J}\!\to\!L_*{<}\infty$.

\paragraph{Entropy proxy and its limits.}
A cheap diagnostic is the harmonic mean of per-query entropy lengths $e^{H_i}$, where $H_i = -\sum_j p_i(j)\log p_i(j)$:
\begin{equation}
\frac{1}{L_{\mathrm{eff}}^{H}} \;=\; \mathbb{E}_{\ell,h,i}\!\left[\,e^{-H_i}\,\right].
\label{eq:leff-H}
\end{equation}
If each active window is approximately flat on support $W_i$ of size $m_i$ and $g_i$ has energy concentrated on $W_i$, then $e^{-H_i}{=}1/m_i$ and $g_i^\top J_{\mathrm{sm}}(p_i) g_i/\|P_{W_i}g_i\|_2^2 = 1/m_i$, giving $L_{\mathrm{eff}}^{J} = L_{\mathrm{eff}}^{H}$. The proxy and theoretical object differ in two regimes. First, if attention has nonuniform masses $p_j = (1{+}\varepsilon_j)/m$ and $g_i$ covaries with $\varepsilon_j$, then the Rayleigh quotient picks up the first-order alignment term $\sum_j \varepsilon_j g_j^2/\sum_j g_j^2$ which $e^{-H}$ lacks. Second, for near-one-hot attention ($H_i{\to}0$), $e^{-H_i}\!\to\!1$ would suggest ``effective length $\approx 1$'', whereas $J_{\mathrm{sm}}(p_i)\to 0$: the softmax saturates and post-softmax sensitivity vanishes, so $L_{\mathrm{eff}}^{J}$ reads sharp attention as low-transport rather than short-but-sensitive. We therefore treat $L_{\mathrm{eff}}^{J}$ as the theoretical quantity and $L_{\mathrm{eff}}^{H}$ as a cheap flat-window proxy.

\paragraph{Perturbative self-consistency of Proposition~\ref{prop:softmax-transport}.} Within the same perturbative softmax-transport model, the replacement $1/L \mapsto 1/L_{\mathrm{eff}}^{J}$ in Proposition~\ref{prop:softmax-transport} is a model-based extension of the diffuse-baseline calculation rather than an empirical shortcut. Writing the log-partition function $A(z) = \log\sum_j e^{z_j}$ and $p_\epsilon = \mathrm{softmax}(z+\epsilon g)$, one has $D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p\|p_\epsilon) = A(z+\epsilon g) - A(z) - \epsilon p^\top g = \tfrac{1}{2}\epsilon^2\, g^\top J_{\mathrm{sm}}(p)\,g + R_3$ with $|R_3|\le C_3|\epsilon|^3\|g\|_\infty^3$. Since the second derivative of $A$ is $J_{\mathrm{sm}}(p) = \mathrm{diag}(p) - pp^\top$, the directional curvature $g^\top J_{\mathrm{sm}}(p)g = \mathrm{Var}_{j\sim p}[g_j]$ depends only on the distribution $p$. For $p_0 = 1/L$ this reduces to $\|Pg\|^2/L$, the unique origin of the $1/L$ factor in the diffuse-baseline derivation. Substituting $1/L \mapsto \mathbb{E}[g^\top J_{\mathrm{sm}}(p) g]/\mathbb{E}[\|Pg\|^2] = 1/L_{\mathrm{eff}}^{J}$ and re-running the small-$\tau$ variational balance $F(\tau) = \tfrac{1}{2}S_{\chi^2}(\tau) - \lambda U(\tau,p)$ gives $\tau_*^2 = 45\lambda Q_1^{J}(p,b)\,d_{\mathrm{head}}^2/L_{\mathrm{eff}}^{J} + O(d_{\mathrm{head}}^4/(L_{\mathrm{eff}}^{J})^2)$, with the residual $R_3$ absorbed into higher-order terms under the assumption $|\tau^2|\cdot\max_j|g_j|$ small. Thus, under the stated perturbative smallness condition, the same structural balance admits the trained-attention substitution; direct measurement of $L_{\mathrm{eff}}^{J}$ is treated as a falsifiable check rather than a theorem for arbitrary trained attention distributions.

\paragraph{Validation protocol.}
No retraining is required for the proxy test. On existing $16$K or $32$K checkpoints, log attention matrices on ${\sim}10^3$ sequences and compute~\eqref{eq:leff-H}. For the theoretical object, evaluate~\eqref{eq:g-closed-form} or~\eqref{eq:g-finite-tau} on the same batch and substitute into~\eqref{eq:leff-ratio-of-means}. A falsifying measurement would be either $L_{\mathrm{eff}}^{J}{\approx}L_{\mathrm{train}}$ with a saturating empirical optimum, or $L_{\mathrm{eff}}^{J}\!\to\!L_*{<}\infty$ with $\tau^{*}\!\propto\!L_{\mathrm{train}}^{-1/2}$ continuing to hold at $L_{\mathrm{train}}{\geq}16$K.

\subsection{Pure-tether branch: Fisher-forcing coefficient and residual bound}
\label{sec:pure-tether-bound}

\paragraph{Setup.} The full stationary ODE under $\mathcal{C}_{\mathrm{app}}$ plus a Fisher utility is $\rho''-\tau^2\rho = \gamma\,b^{-2\phi}$. Writing $s{=}2\log b$ and $q_s(\phi){=}e^{-s\phi}$, the pure-tether branch $\rho_{\mathrm{EVQ}}(\phi) = \tau\cosh(\tau(1{-}\phi))/\sinh\tau$ solves the homogeneous problem; the full solution is $\rho_{\mathrm{full}} = \rho_{\mathrm{EVQ}} + \delta\rho_{F}$.

\paragraph{Explicit form of $\gamma(b)$.} The Fisher utility in the Euler equation $\alpha\rho + \beta(G\rho) - \lambda_F V_b + \nu = 0$ has $V_b(\phi) = \eta_F(\phi)\,b^{-2\phi}$, where $\eta_F(\phi)$ is the activation-conditioned Fisher coefficient with
\begin{equation}
\eta_F(\phi_k) \;=\; \frac{1}{2\,s_{\mathrm{att}}^2}\,\mathbb{E}_{\ell,h,i,j}\!\left[\,w_{\ell h ij}\,|\alpha_{\ell h ij,k}|^2\,\right],\qquad \alpha_{\ell h ij,k} \;=\; q_{i,k}^{\mathbb{C}}\,\overline{k_{j,k}^{\mathbb{C}}}.
\label{eq:eta-F}
\end{equation}
Using $(G\rho)''{=}-\rho$, differentiating the Euler equation twice gives $\rho'' - \tau^2\rho = (\lambda_F/\alpha) V_b''$. For slowly varying $\eta_F$ compared with $e^{-s\phi}$,
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;\gamma(b) \;=\; \frac{\lambda_F}{\alpha}\,\bar\eta_F\,(2\log b)^2 \;+\; O\!\left(\tfrac{|\eta_F'|}{s\bar\eta_F} + \tfrac{|\eta_F''|}{s^2\bar\eta_F}\right).\;}
\label{eq:gamma-explicit}
\end{equation}
The full $\eta_F(\phi)$-local version is $\gamma_{\mathrm{loc}}(\phi;b) = (\lambda_F/\alpha)[\eta_F''(\phi) - 2s\eta_F'(\phi) + s^2\eta_F(\phi)]$.

\paragraph{Suppression is L$^1$/CDF, not pointwise.} The particular solution $\delta\rho_{F}^{\mathrm{part}} = \gamma(b)\,q_s/(s^2-\tau^2)$ combined with~\eqref{eq:gamma-explicit} gives pointwise amplitude
\begin{equation}
\delta\rho_{F}^{\mathrm{part}}(\phi) \;\approx\; \frac{\lambda_F\bar\eta_F}{\alpha}\,\frac{s^2}{s^2-\tau^2}\,e^{-s\phi},
\label{eq:delta-rho-F-pointwise}
\end{equation}
so the $(s^2-\tau^2)^{-1}$ factor is cancelled by the $s^2$ in $\gamma(b)$, and $\delta\rho_{F}^{\mathrm{part}}$ is \emph{not} pointwise small in $s$. The true suppression comes from the boundary-layer width of $q_s$: $\|q_s\|_1 = (1-e^{-s})/s = O(1/\log b)$ and $\|q_s\|_2 = O(1/\sqrt{\log b})$. Hence
\begin{align}
\|\rho_{\mathrm{full}}-\rho_{\mathrm{EVQ}}\|_1 \;&\le\; C_{\mathrm{bc}}^{(1)}(\tau)\;\frac{\lambda_F\bar\eta_F}{\alpha}\,\frac{s^2}{s^2-\tau^2}\,\frac{1-e^{-s}}{s} \;=\; O\!\left(\tfrac{1}{\log b}\right),\label{eq:tether-L1-correct}\\
\|\rho_{\mathrm{full}}-\rho_{\mathrm{EVQ}}\|_2 \;&\le\; C_{\mathrm{bc}}^{(2)}(\tau)\;\frac{\lambda_F\bar\eta_F}{\alpha}\,\frac{s^2}{s^2-\tau^2}\,\sqrt{\tfrac{1-e^{-2s}}{2s}} \;=\; O\!\left(\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{\log b}}\right),
\label{eq:tether-L2-correct}
\end{align}
and the CDF-level error is $\|F_{\mathrm{full}}-F_{\mathrm{EVQ}}\|_\infty \le \|\delta\rho_{F}\|_1 = O(1/\log b)$. Fisher forcing is therefore globally small in CDF / $L^1$ transport at large $b$, but not necessarily pointwise small near $\phi{=}0$.

\paragraph{Warp amplification at large $\tau$.} Via the inverse-CDF map,
\begin{equation}
\|\phi_{\mathrm{full}}-\phi_{\mathrm{EVQ}}\|_\infty \;\le\; \frac{\|\delta\rho_{F}\|_1}{\inf_\phi \rho_{\mathrm{full}}(\phi)}, \qquad \inf_\phi \rho_{\mathrm{EVQ}}(\phi) \;=\; \frac{\tau}{\sinh\tau},
\label{eq:warp-bound}
\end{equation}
so CDF perturbations are amplified by $\sinh\tau/\tau$ when translated into warp errors; very short $L$ or very large $d_{\mathrm{head}}$ should re-include the forcing branch.

\paragraph{Dimensionless diagnostic.} Using $\|\rho_{\mathrm{EVQ}}-1\|_2 = \tau^2/\sqrt{45}+O(\tau^4)$ and $\Gamma_b \coloneqq (\lambda_F\bar\eta_F/\alpha)\,s^2/((s^2-\tau^2)\tau^2)$, the $L^2$-level ratio is
\begin{equation}
R_F \;\coloneqq\; \frac{\|\rho_{\mathrm{full}}-\rho_{\mathrm{EVQ}}\|_2}{\|\rho_{\mathrm{EVQ}}-1\|_2} \;\le\; C_{\mathrm{bc}}^{(2)}\,\Gamma_b\,\sqrt{45}\,\sqrt{\tfrac{1-e^{-2s}}{2s}} \;=\; O\!\left(\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{\log b}}\right).
\label{eq:RF}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Second-order utility deficit.} Writing the surrogate objective as a quadratic form with operator $A_\tau$, the full branch improves the objective by
\begin{equation}
\Delta_F \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\gamma(b)^2\,\langle q_s^\perp,\,A_\tau^{-1} q_s^\perp\rangle,
\label{eq:tether-deficit}
\end{equation}
so dropping the forcing term is $O(\gamma(b)^2)$: if $R_F\!\le\!r$, the missed correction is $O(r^2)$ relative to the tether deformation energy.

\paragraph{Practical rule.} Retain the forcing branch whenever the measured $R_F$ exceeds a tolerance $r_{\mathrm{tol}}$ (we use $0.05$ for training, $0.01$ for theory plots), or when $\tau/(2\log b)$ is not small. The numerical threshold depends on the paper's Fisher normalization $\lambda_F\bar\eta_F/\alpha$, which we treat as a measurable constant rather than a derived value.

\subsection{Channel-load motivation for Pearson \texorpdfstring{$\chi^2$}{chi2}}
\label{sec:chi2-load}

\paragraph{Normalization convention.} Throughout this paper, $S_{\chi^2}(\tau)$ denotes the $d_{\mathrm{head}}$-normalized stiffness $S_{\chi^2}(\tau){:=}\frac{1}{d_{\mathrm{head}}}\!\int_0^1\!(1{-}\rho_\tau)^2/\rho_\tau\,d\phi$, with leading expansion $\tau^4/(45\,d_{\mathrm{head}}){+}O(\tau^6)$ consistent with Proposition~\ref{prop:softmax-transport}. Identities below that produce a bare $\tau^4/45$ leading coefficient apply to $d_{\mathrm{head}}\,S_{\chi^2}(\tau)$ (the un-normalized integral); the $d_{\mathrm{head}}$ factor enters via the surrogate diagonal $\alpha{=}1/d_{\mathrm{rot}}$ ($={}1/d_{\mathrm{head}}$ for MHA via \S\ref{sec:tau-scaling}; ${=}1/d_{\mathrm{rot}}$ for compressed-RoPE MLA via \S\ref{sec:mla-results}).

\paragraph{Channel-load axioms.} Inverse-CDF quantization places channels with cell width $\ell(\phi){=}1/\rho(\phi)$ and samples channels according to $dP_\rho{=}\rho\,d\phi$. Under the modeling axioms (i)~channel-measure averaging, (ii)~load $\ell$ as the primitive local quantity, (iii)~second central moment, (iv)~uniform density has zero stiffness, (v)~local additivity, the canonical load-variance penalty is
\begin{equation}
S(\rho) \;=\; \mathrm{Var}_{\phi\sim\rho\,d\phi}\!\left[\,\tfrac{1}{\rho(\phi)}\,\right] \;=\; \int_0^1 \frac{(1-\rho(\phi))^2}{\rho(\phi)}\,d\phi \;=\; S_{\chi^2}(\rho).
\label{eq:chi2-load-identity}
\end{equation}
We treat this as a canonical motivated choice under the stated axioms, not a uniqueness claim.

\paragraph{Load-moment exponent.} Distinct from the weighted-$L^2$ family $S_p(\tau){=}\frac{1}{d_{\mathrm{head}}}\int(\rho_\tau{-}1)^2/\rho_\tau^p\,d\phi$ used in Table~\ref{tab:stiffness-sweep} (where Pearson $\chi^2$ is $p{=}1$), we also examine a load-moment family $S^{\mathrm{mom}}_p(\rho){=}\mathbb{E}_\rho[(1/\rho{-}1)^p]$. Substituting the EVQ cosh density and minimizing $S^{\mathrm{mom}}_p{-}\lambda U$ gives $\tau^{2(p-1)}\asymp d_{\mathrm{head}}^2/L_{\mathrm{eff}}^{J}$, hence $\tau^{*}\propto (L_{\mathrm{eff}}^{J})^{-1/(2p-2)}$; only $p{=}2$ yields the observed $L^{-1/2}$ exponent within this family. The two families parametrize different $f$-divergence neighborhoods and need not match on the finite-$\tau$ grid.

\paragraph{$f$-divergences at finite $\tau$.} Smooth $f$-divergences (KL, Jeffreys, R\'enyi) are locally quadratic around $\rho{=}1$ and share the leading $-1/2$ exponent asymptotically. Their \emph{finite-$\tau$} best points over the $L\!\in\![128,4096]$ grid used in Table~\ref{tab:stiffness-sweep} fit exponents in $[0.58,0.81]$, outside the $\pm 1\%$ PPL basin around $\gamma{=}0.500$ at the tested scales.

\subsection{\texorpdfstring{$\lambda$}{lambda} as a curvature ratio}
\label{sec:lambda-curvature}

\paragraph{Invariant definition.} Fix the normalizations of $U_{\mathrm{unit}}$ and $U_{\mathrm{exact}}$ in the $\theta=\tau^2$ expansion. Define
\begin{equation}
\lambda_\infty
\;\coloneqq\;
\frac{\partial_\theta U_{\mathrm{exact}}(\rho_{\sqrt{\theta}};L,b)|_{\theta=0}}
     {\partial_\theta U_{\mathrm{unit}}(\rho_{\sqrt{\theta}};L,b)|_{\theta=0}}
\;=\; \frac{u_2^{\mathrm{exact}}}{u_2^{\mathrm{unit}}}.
\label{eq:lambda-true}
\end{equation}
Writing $U_{\mathrm{exact}}=u_2^{\mathrm{exact}}(L,b)\,(d_{\mathrm{head}}/L_{\mathrm{eff}}^{J})\tau^2 + O(\tau^4)$ makes $\lambda_\infty$ the utility-curvature ratio absorbed by the unit convention. The stiffness side remains $d_{\mathrm{head}}\,S_{\chi^2}(\tau) = (\sinh\tau/\tau^2)\arctan(\sinh\tau) - 1 = \tau^4/45 + O(\tau^6)$ (cf.\ ``Normalization convention'' in \S\ref{sec:chi2-load}).

\paragraph{A posteriori consistency check.} Under the $\lambda{=}1$ unit convention, the surrogate prediction is $c_{\mathrm{pred}}(L,b) = \sqrt{45\,Q_1(L,b)}$. Direct numerical integration of $Q_1(L,b) = \int_0^1 a(\phi) q(Lb^{-\phi})\,d\phi$ at $b{=}500\mathrm{K}$, $L\in\{256,512,1024\}$ gives $Q_1 \in [0.0314, 0.0319]$ (near-constant on this grid), hence $c_{\mathrm{pred}}\approx 1.19$. The exact-kernel collision minimizer $c_{\mathrm{coll}}{=}1.171\pm 0.001$ (9 configurations, Table~\ref{tab:lambda-cv}) agrees with this to within $2\%$ (mean $c_{\mathrm{coll}}/c_{\mathrm{pred}}{=}0.981\pm 0.003$, CV $0.28\%$), so $(c_{\mathrm{coll}}/c_{\mathrm{pred}})^2 = 0.96\pm 0.005$ is within $\sim 4\%$ of unity. This is an \emph{a posteriori} consistency check for the $\lambda{=}1$ convention inside the flat empirical basin; we do not treat $\lambda$ as an independently derived physical constant.

\paragraph{Validation protocol (further).} A tighter check would compute $u_2^{\mathrm{exact}}=\partial_{\tau^2}U_{\mathrm{exact}}(\rho_\tau;L,b)|_{\tau=0}$ directly from the oscillatory RoPE kernel and compare against $u_2^{\mathrm{unit}}=Q_1(L,b)$; the collision-score proxy above suggests the ratio is already within $4\%$ of unity across our configurations.

\subsection{Discrete-channel transport gap and the MLA channel-scarcity mechanism}
\label{sec:discrete-continuous-gap}

\paragraph{Setup.} Inverse-CDF quantization replaces the continuous density $\rho$ by an atomic measure. Let $F(\phi){=}\int_0^\phi\rho$, $Q(u){=}F^{-1}(u)$, midpoint grid $u_k{=}(k{-}\tfrac{1}{2})/K$, and $\phi_k{=}Q(u_k)$. The empirical channel measure is $\mu_K{=}K^{-1}\sum_{k=1}^{K}\delta_{\phi_k}$. Direct $\|\rho_K-\rho\|$ is ill-posed: one cannot subtract an atomic measure from a density. We therefore work in Wasserstein distance for transport and in a quantile-cell histogram $\rho_K$ for density.

\paragraph{Transport bounds.} Assume $0<m\le\rho\le M$ and $\|\rho'\|_\infty\le B$; then $Q$ is $(1/m)$-Lipschitz. The midpoint quantization errors satisfy
\begin{equation}
W_\infty(\mu_K,\mu_\rho) \;\le\; \frac{1}{2Km}, \qquad W_1(\mu_K,\mu_\rho) \;\le\; \frac{1}{4Km}.
\label{eq:wasserstein-bound}
\end{equation}
Defining the quantile-cell histogram $\rho_K(\phi){=}1/(K|I_k|)$ on $I_k{=}[Q((k{-}1)/K),\,Q(k/K)]$, the histogram density satisfies
\begin{equation}
\|\rho_K-\rho\|_1 \;\le\; \frac{B}{Km}, \qquad \|\rho_K-\rho\|_\infty \;\le\; \frac{B}{Km}.
\label{eq:density-bound}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Application to EVQ-Cosh.} For $\rho_\tau(\phi)=\tau\cosh(\tau(1{-}\phi))/\sinh\tau$, $m_\tau=\tau/\sinh\tau$ and $B_\tau=\tau^2$. Substituting,
\begin{equation}
W_1(\mu_{K,\tau},\mu_{\rho_\tau}) \;\le\; \frac{\sinh\tau}{4K\tau}, \qquad \|\rho_{K,\tau}-\rho_\tau\|_1 \;\le\; \frac{\tau\sinh\tau}{K} \;=\; \frac{\tau^2}{K}+O\!\left(\tfrac{\tau^4}{K}\right).
\label{eq:evq-discrete-bound}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Kernel-integrated errors.} For a smooth two-variable kernel $K_{\mathrm{sm}}$ with Lipschitz constant $L_K$ in each argument,
\begin{equation}
\left|\iint K_{\mathrm{sm}}\,d\mu_K\,d\mu_K - \iint K_{\mathrm{sm}}\,d\mu_\rho\,d\mu_\rho\right| \;\le\; \frac{L_K}{2Km} + O(K^{-2}).
\label{eq:kernel-bound}
\end{equation}
The singular $\delta$-component of $K_{\mathrm{app}}$ must be interpreted as a cell average (not as a literal atom-kernel pairing), after which the bound applies to the smoothed kernel.

\paragraph{High-resolution distortion.} For a smooth weighted distortion $w$,
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{D}_K[\rho] \;\approx\; \frac{1}{12K^2}\int_0^1 \frac{w(\phi)}{\rho(\phi)^2}\,d\phi + O(K^{-3}).
\label{eq:high-res-distortion}
\end{equation}
This is the finite-channel mechanism: a shaped $\rho$ reduces the weighted inverse-density load $\int w/\rho^2$; the absolute benefit scales as $K^{-2}$ for smooth quantization error and as $K^{-1}$ for transport/kernel terms.

\paragraph{Channel scarcity amplifies the density-shape correction.} Going from standard MHA ($K{=}64$) to MLA ($K{=}16$, i.e.\ $d_{\mathrm{rope}}{=}32$) scales the $K^{-2}$ component of the distortion bound by a factor of $16$ and the $K^{-1}$ component by a factor of $4$. The observed PPL-gain ratio between MLA ($31.1\%$ at $K{=}16$; \S\ref{sec:exp-mla}) and MHA ($-13.3\%$ at $K{=}64$, 3-seed 454M FineWeb-Edu row in Table~\ref{tab:multiscale}; with 1-seed rows ranging $-10.9\%$ to $-18.9\%$) is approximately $2{\times}$: this is directionally consistent with the predicted $K^{-1}$/$K^{-2}$ amplification but is not a quantitative match, since the realized PPL gain is moderated by architecture, optimization dynamics, and loss nonlinearities that the surrogate does not model. The bounds upgrade the Class~C2 observation from a qualitative ``channels-scarce regime amplifies shape correction'' to an explicit $K^{-1}$/$K^{-2}$ scaling on the surrogate distortion.

\paragraph{Caveat.} At large $\tau$ (short $L$ or large $d_{\mathrm{head}}$), $m_\tau{=}\tau/\sinh\tau$ becomes small and the $1/(Km_\tau)$ factor grows, so these bounds become vacuous in the very-large-$\tau$ regime where the pure-tether branch itself needs re-inclusion of the forcing term (\S\ref{sec:pure-tether-bound}).
