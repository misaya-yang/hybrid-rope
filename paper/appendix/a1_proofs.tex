\section{Proof Details}
\label{sec:proofs}

\subsection{From the broadband surrogate to the ODE}

Starting from the surrogate kernel
\begin{equation}
K_{\mathrm{app}}(\phi,\psi)=\alpha\delta(\phi-\psi)+\beta\min(\phi,\psi),
\end{equation}
the functional is
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{J}[\rho]
=
\frac{\alpha}{2}\int_0^1 \rho(\phi)^2\,d\phi
+
\frac{\beta}{2}\int_0^1\int_0^1 \rho(\phi)\rho(\psi)\min(\phi,\psi)\,d\phi\,d\psi
- \mu\int_0^1 \rho(\phi)b^{-2\phi}\,d\phi.
\end{equation}
Its first variation is
\begin{equation}
\delta\mathcal{J}
=
\int_0^1
\left[
\alpha\rho(\phi)
+
\beta\int_0^1 \rho(\psi)\min(\phi,\psi)\,d\psi
- \mu b^{-2\phi}
\right]\delta\rho(\phi)\,d\phi.
\end{equation}
The Euler-Lagrange stationarity condition is therefore
\begin{equation}
\alpha\rho(\phi)
+
\beta\int_0^1 \rho(\psi)\min(\phi,\psi)\,d\psi
=
\mu b^{-2\phi}.
\end{equation}
Define
\begin{equation}
g(\phi)=\int_0^1 \rho(\psi)\min(\phi,\psi)\,d\psi
=
\int_0^\phi \rho(\psi)\psi\,d\psi
+
\phi\int_\phi^1 \rho(\psi)\,d\psi.
\end{equation}
Direct differentiation gives
\begin{align}
g'(\phi)
&=
\int_\phi^1 \rho(\psi)\,d\psi,\\
g''(\phi)
&=
-\rho(\phi).
\end{align}
Hence the stationarity equation can be rewritten as
\begin{equation}
\alpha \rho(\phi)+\beta g(\phi)=\mu b^{-2\phi}.
\end{equation}
Differentiating twice with respect to \(\phi\) and substituting \(g''(\phi)=-\rho(\phi)\) yields
\begin{equation}
\rho''(\phi)-\frac{\beta}{\alpha}\rho(\phi)=\gamma b^{-2\phi},
\qquad
\tau=\sqrt{\beta/\alpha}.
\end{equation}
The same calculation shows that \(g(0)=0\) and \(g'(1)=0\), so \(\min(\phi,\psi)\) acts as the Green kernel of \(-d^2/d\phi^2\) under the mixed boundary conditions induced by the surrogate formulation. Conditional on the surrogate, this step is exact.

\subsection{Closed-form solution and geometric limit}

The non-resonant ODE has the closed-form solution
\begin{equation}
\rho^*(\phi)=C_1\cosh(\tau\phi)+C_2\sinh(\tau\phi)+P\,b^{-2\phi}.
\end{equation}
The first two terms span the homogeneous solution of \(\rho''-\tau^2\rho=0\), and the final term is a particular solution induced by the Fisher-weighted source \(b^{-2\phi}\). This decomposition is the source of the main paper's ``tether + pulse'' interpretation.

To translate the density into RoPE frequencies, write the cumulative map
\begin{equation}
F(\phi)=\int_0^\phi \rho^*(s)\,ds,
\end{equation}
normalize it to \([0,1]\), and invert at the discrete quantiles \(u_k=k/K\). In the pure-tether branch this inversion has the closed form
\begin{equation}
\phi_k(\tau)
=
1-\frac{1}{\tau}\operatorname{arcsinh}\!\left((1-u_k)\sinh\tau\right).
\end{equation}
To recover geometric RoPE, expand \(\sinh\tau\approx\tau\) as \(\tau\to 0\). Then
\begin{equation}
\phi_k(\tau)\to 1-\frac{1}{\tau}\operatorname{arcsinh}((1-u_k)\tau)\to u_k,
\end{equation}
which is exactly the uniform quantile map.

This also shows why the high-frequency end remains effectively anchored: for \(u_0=0\), one has \(\phi_0(\tau)=0\) exactly, so the leading inverse frequency remains \(b^0=1\), matching geometric RoPE.

\subsection{Why the pure-tether branch}

The full stationary density contains three terms: a hyperbolic tether (\(\cosh, \sinh\)) and a particular solution \(Pb^{-2\phi}\). The practical EVQ method uses only the pure-tether branch. This is a deliberate design choice, not an oversight, and is justified on three grounds.

\textbf{(1) Separation of concerns.} The homogeneous solution captures the global allocation structure --- how frequency mass should be distributed across the spectrum to minimize broadband collision. The particular solution \(Pb^{-2\phi}\) captures a local Fisher-utility correction that preserves the geometric profile's per-channel information content. In the regime where collision reduction is the binding constraint (i.e., when many frequency channels are wasted by the geometric allocation), the global allocation term dominates the quality improvement, and the local correction is second-order.

\textbf{(2) Closed-form invertibility.} The pure-tether density \(\rho(\phi)\propto\cosh(\tau(1-\phi))\) admits an analytically invertible CDF, yielding the closed-form warp \(\phi_k(\tau)\). Including the \(b^{-2\phi}\) term destroys this invertibility and requires numerical root-finding, sacrificing the method's main practical advantage: a deterministic, parameter-free initialization formula.

\textbf{(3) The forcing term is amplitude-suppressed.} The particular solution coefficient $P = \gamma/(4\ln^2 b - \tau^2)$ is strongly suppressed at typical base values: $P \propto 1/687$ at $b{=}500\mathrm{K}$ and $1/337$ at $b{=}10\mathrm{K}$. We verify numerically by computing collision scores under the exact kernel for the full density $\rho(\phi) = \cosh(\tau(1{-}\phi)) + \lambda b^{-2\phi}$ across $\lambda \in [0, 5]$. At the theoretically motivated $\lambda \approx P \approx 0.001$--$0.003$, the full solution improves over pure tether by $<0.5\%$. Even at artificially large $\lambda{=}1$, the improvement is only $2$--$6\%$ across configurations (vs.\ the $28$--$92\%$ improvement over geometric). The pure tether already captures the vast majority of the collision reduction.

\textbf{(4) Empirical validation of the design choice.} The pure-tether branch subsumes geometric RoPE as its \(\tau\to 0\) limit, and the 99-run Phase~16 sweep (Figure~\ref{fig:tau-sweep}) confirms that the optimal \(\tau\) in this family matches the scaling-law prediction across multiple model scales. If the omitted forcing term were essential, one would expect the pure-tether family to systematically underperform --- but in fact it consistently improves over geometric at the predicted \(\tau^*\). The forcing term may further improve performance, but this is an avenue for future work rather than a deficiency of the current method.

\subsection{Surrogate quality: functional validation}
\label{sec:surrogate-validation}

The broadband surrogate $K_{\mathrm{app}} = \alpha\delta + \beta\min$ is the theory's sole approximation. We validate it not by pointwise comparison with the exact kernel $K(\phi_1,\phi_2) = \int D(\Delta)\cos(\omega(\phi_1)\Delta)\cos(\omega(\phi_2)\Delta)\,d\Delta$ --- which is oscillatory (sinc-like) and cannot be well-approximated pointwise by a smooth two-parameter model --- but by the \emph{functional} test: does the EVQ allocation derived from $K_{\mathrm{app}}$ also reduce the collision objective under the true kernel $K$?

Table~\ref{tab:surrogate-validation} answers affirmatively. For a uniform distance prior $D(\Delta) = 1/L$ on $[0, L]$, we compute the exact kernel $K$ at the discrete allocation points for both geometric and EVQ, then evaluate the normalized collision score $C = \sum_{i<j} K_{ij}^2/(K_{ii}K_{jj})$ and effective spectral rank (exponential of the Shannon entropy of the eigenvalue distribution). We sweep across six training lengths ($L \in \{128, \ldots, 4096\}$), three base values ($b \in \{10\mathrm{K}, 100\mathrm{K}, 500\mathrm{K}\}$), and two channel counts ($K \in \{16, 32\}$), covering both text and video regimes.

EVQ reduces collision under the exact kernel in all 12 configurations ($-24\%$ to $-92\%$), with effective rank improvements of $+24\%$ to $+570\%$. Two patterns emerge. First, the collision reduction monotonically increases as the geometric allocation becomes more degenerate: shorter $L$ (more extrapolation stress at fixed $\tau^*$) and larger $b$ (more dead channels) both amplify the advantage, consistent with the dead-channel mechanism. Second, even in the most favorable regime for geometric RoPE ($L{=}4096$, where baseline effective rank is already $6.6$), EVQ still improves collision by $-24\%$, confirming that the surrogate identifies a genuine structural improvement across the entire parameter space, not only in extreme regimes.

\subsection{Waterbed assumptions}

The waterbed proposition in the body is conditional. It requires the surrogate regime in which the relevant error functional is strictly positive and logarithmically integrable. Under those assumptions, Jensen's inequality yields a lower bound of the form
\begin{equation}
\int \ln E(\phi)\,d\phi \ge \ln b - \ln c,
\end{equation}
with equality at the geometric allocation. The main paper therefore states Waterbed as a proposition with explicit assumptions rather than as an unconditional theorem.

The logic is the standard convexity argument. If the normalized frequency-density reallocation is written as \(\rho(\phi)\) with \(\int_0^1 \rho(\phi)\,d\phi=1\), then geometric RoPE corresponds to the uniform density. The logarithmic error volume is minimized when the reallocation is uniform, and any non-uniform redistribution must increase the global log-volume even if it reduces error on a task-relevant sub-band. This is exactly the tradeoff needed for the main-text claim that EVQ can improve long-range precision while paying a bounded in-range cost.

\subsection{Scaling analysis of \texorpdfstring{$\tau^*$}{tau*}: from surrogate to empirical law}
\label{sec:tau-scaling}

The scaling law
\begin{equation}
\tau^* = \frac{d_{\mathrm{head}}}{\sqrt{L}}
\end{equation}
is validated across 99 training runs (27 configurations $\times$ 3+ seeds, Figure~\ref{fig:tau-sweep}). Here we analyze to what extent the broadband surrogate predicts this formula, and identify the gap that remains.

\paragraph{Numerical characterization of surrogate coefficients.}
We fit $K_{\mathrm{app}}(\phi_i,\phi_j) = \alpha\delta_{ij}/\Delta\phi + \beta\min(\phi_i,\phi_j)$ to the exact collision kernel $K(\phi_i,\phi_j) = \frac{1}{2L}\bigl[\frac{\sin((\omega_1{-}\omega_2)L)}{\omega_1{-}\omega_2} + \frac{\sin((\omega_1{+}\omega_2)L)}{\omega_1{+}\omega_2}\bigr]$ evaluated at $K = d_{\mathrm{head}}/2$ uniformly spaced channel positions in $[0,1]$. Sweeping across $L \in \{128,\ldots,4096\}$, $d_{\mathrm{head}} \in \{32,64,128,256\}$, and $b \in \{10\mathrm{K}, 100\mathrm{K}, 500\mathrm{K}\}$ yields:
\begin{equation}
\alpha \;\approx\; \frac{1}{2K} \;=\; \frac{1}{d_{\mathrm{head}}},
\qquad
\beta \;\sim\; L^{-0.22},\; O(1).
\end{equation}
The diagonal coefficient $\alpha$ is set by the kernel's diagonal value ($K(\phi,\phi) \approx 1/2$ for most channels) times the channel spacing $\Delta\phi = 1/K$. Crucially, $\alpha$ depends on $d_{\mathrm{head}}$ through the discretization ($K = d_{\mathrm{head}}/2$) and is nearly independent of $L$. The off-diagonal coefficient $\beta$ has weak power-law decay with $L$ and negligible $d_{\mathrm{head}}$ dependence.

\paragraph{What the surrogate predicts.}
Substituting into $\tau_{\mathrm{surr}} = \sqrt{\beta/\alpha}$:
\begin{equation}
\tau_{\mathrm{surr}} = \sqrt{\beta \cdot d_{\mathrm{head}}} \;\sim\; \sqrt{d_{\mathrm{head}}} \cdot L^{-0.11}.
\end{equation}
This correctly captures the $d_{\mathrm{head}}$ dependence: $\alpha \propto 1/d_{\mathrm{head}}$ implies $\tau \propto \sqrt{d_{\mathrm{head}}}$, matching the direction and monotonicity of the empirical law. However, the $L$-scaling is too weak: $L^{-0.11}$ vs.\ the empirical $L^{-0.5}$.

\paragraph{The discrete--continuous gap.}
The discrepancy arises because the broadband surrogate operates in a \emph{continuous} function space, where the number of channels $K$ appears only through the integral constraint $\int\rho = K$. The surrogate's optimal $\tau$ thus captures the infinite-resolution limit; it does not account for the finite-channel oscillatory correlations that penalize over-concentration at low frequencies.

To see why the continuous theory underestimates the $L$-penalty, consider the exact kernel's off-diagonal structure. For two channels with similar log-frequencies ($\phi_1 \approx \phi_2$), the exact kernel contains an oscillatory term $\sin((\omega_1{-}\omega_2)L)/(\omega_1{-}\omega_2)$ that produces constructive interference when $|\omega_1{-}\omega_2|L \ll \pi$. Packing more channels near the low-frequency end (large $\tau$) reduces inter-channel frequency separation, pushing neighboring channels into this constructive-interference regime. The resulting excess correlation acts as an implicit penalty on large $\tau$ that the smooth $\beta\min(\phi_1,\phi_2)$ surrogate does not capture.

This penalty grows with $L$: longer sequences create more phase diversity per channel, making the oscillatory inter-channel terms more sensitive to frequency crowding. The strengthened $L$-dependence converts $\tau_{\mathrm{surr}} \sim \sqrt{d_{\mathrm{head}}} \cdot L^{-0.11}$ into $\tau^* = d_{\mathrm{head}}/\sqrt{L}$.

Confirming this picture, we verified numerically that at the special point $d_{\mathrm{head}} = L$ (where the number of encoding dimensions equals the number of positions, and the continuous and discrete problems coincide), the surrogate formula $\tau_{\mathrm{surr}} = \sqrt{\beta/\alpha}$ matches $d_{\mathrm{head}}/\sqrt{L}$ to within $3\%$. As the ratio $L/d_{\mathrm{head}}$ increases, the surrogate progressively overestimates $\tau^*$, consistent with a growing discrete-correction term.

\paragraph{Summary.}
The broadband surrogate provides two of the three ingredients for $\tau^*$: the \emph{functional form} (cosh density, via the ODE) and the \emph{$d_{\mathrm{head}}$ dependence} (via $\alpha = 1/d_{\mathrm{head}}$). The $L^{-1/2}$ exponent is a finite-channel correction that the continuous variational problem does not resolve. Together, these yield
\begin{equation}
\tau^* = \frac{d_{\mathrm{head}}}{\sqrt{L}},
\end{equation}
validated empirically with $R^2 > 0.99$ across 99 runs (Figure~\ref{fig:tau-sweep}). Deriving the exact $L^{-1/2}$ exponent from a discrete variational principle remains an open mathematical question. The body therefore maintains the ``empirical law'' designation while acknowledging the partial theoretical support.
