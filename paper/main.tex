\title{EVQ-Cosh: Variational Frequency Allocation for Rotary Position Embedding}

\author{Anonymous Authors}

\begin{document}

\begin{abstract}
Rotary position embedding (RoPE) is usually designed along two axes: the base frequency and inference-time position rescaling. We study a third axis, the training-time allocation of the $d_{\mathrm{head}}/2$ frequency channels across the spectrum. We formulate this allocation as a variational inverse problem over log-frequency densities and derive \textsc{EVQ-cosh}, a closed-form one-parameter family in which geometric RoPE is the $\tau{=}0$ boundary under a broadband functional surrogate of the phase-collision kernel. EVQ-cosh changes only the inverse-frequency initialization and adds zero learned parameters. A semi-analytic operating-point rule, $\tau^{*}{=}d_{\mathrm{head}}/\sqrt{L}$, selects a non-geometric allocation; its $d_{\mathrm{head}}$ and $L^{-1/2}$ structure is derived, and the remaining $\mathcal{O}(1)$ normalization is validated as lying inside a flat empirical PPL basin. Our primary evidence is tiered: in PE-dominant DAPE-style $128{\to}8\mathrm{K}$ extrapolation, EVQ improves over geometric RoPE and a learned positional operator; under matched YaRN scale, EVQ increases YaRN leverage on a $454$M passkey-mix model, reaching $100\%$ passkey retrieval at $8\mathrm{K}$ while Geo+YaRN remains at $61\%$; and in compressed-RoPE MLA with $16$ frequency channels, EVQ gives the largest gains. These results support training-time frequency allocation \emph{shape}, not only frequency \emph{range}, as a first-order RoPE design axis.
\end{abstract}

\section*{Broader Impact}

This work studies frequency allocation as a design axis for rotary position embedding rather than a specific deployed application. Its main positive impact is scientific and systems-oriented: replacing the default geometric frequency schedule with a principled variational allocation can reduce wasted compute and improve the reliability of long-context models across architectures. The main risk is over-generalization. Our strongest evidence remains concentrated on text extrapolation and retrieval-heavy long-context evaluation, so we do not present EVQ as a universal guarantee of downstream improvement across all tasks, modalities, or deployment settings.

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Question:} Do the main claims made in the abstract and introduction accurately reflect the paper's contributions and scope?
\item \textbf{Answer:} \answerYes{}
