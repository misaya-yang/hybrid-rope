\title{EVQ-Cosh: Optimal Frequency Allocation for Rotary Position Embedding}

\author{Anonymous Authors}

\begin{document}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Rotary position embedding (RoPE) is governed by three design axes: the base frequency, the head dimension, and the allocation of frequencies across channels. The first two have received significant attention; the third---how frequency mass is distributed---has remained fixed at the geometric (equal-ratio) schedule since RoFormer, despite being a key determinant of positional representational quality. We formulate frequency allocation as a variational inverse problem, derive a broadband surrogate whose stationary density satisfies a closed-form ODE, and obtain a one-parameter family (EVQ-cosh) in which geometric RoPE is the degenerate \(\tau{=}0\) limit. EVQ is architecture-agnostic: it modifies only the inverse-frequency initialization, adds zero parameters, and composes with existing inference-time methods. On standard multi-head attention (MHA), EVQ+YaRN reaches \(100\%\) retrieval at \(8\mathrm{K}\) (3~seeds per configuration, 6~total) where Geo+YaRN plateaus at \(61\%\), and lowers PPL by \(14\text{--}32\%\) at \(8\text{--}16\mathrm{K}\). On Multi-head Latent Attention (MLA, DeepSeek-V2/V3), where only 16 frequency channels carry positional information, EVQ alone outperforms Geo+YaRN at \(2\times\) extrapolation (\(-31.1\%\) PPL, 3~seeds). In PE-dominant extreme extrapolation, EVQ surpasses learnable positional encoding with zero extra parameters (\(333.7\) vs.\ \(455.3\) PPL). These results identify frequency allocation as a fundamental and under-explored design axis for rotary position embedding, with consistent gains across MHA, MLA, and video DiT architectures.
\end{abstract}

\section*{Broader Impact}

This work studies frequency allocation as a design axis for rotary position embedding rather than a specific deployed application. Its main positive impact is scientific and systems-oriented: replacing the default geometric frequency schedule with a principled variational allocation can reduce wasted compute and improve the reliability of long-context models across architectures. The main risk is over-generalization. Our strongest evidence remains concentrated on text extrapolation and retrieval-heavy long-context evaluation, so we do not present EVQ as a universal guarantee of downstream improvement across all tasks, modalities, or deployment settings.
