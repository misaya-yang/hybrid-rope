\title{EVQ-Cosh: Variational Frequency Allocation for Rotary Position Embedding}

\author{Anonymous Authors}

\begin{document}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Rotary position embedding (RoPE) is not only a positional operator but also a finite spectral budget: each head has only $d_{\mathrm{head}}/2$ rotary frequency channels, yet the standard geometric schedule spends this budget by inherited convention. We study training-time frequency-channel allocation as a RoPE design axis complementary to operator design and inference-time range scaling. Formulating allocation as a variational inverse problem over log-frequency densities, we derive \textsc{EVQ-Cosh}, a closed-form one-parameter family in which geometric RoPE is recovered as the $\tau{\to}0$ limit of the pure-tether sub-family under a broadband collision surrogate. EVQ-Cosh changes only the inverse-frequency initialization and adds zero learned parameters. A semi-analytic operating-point rule $\tau^{*}{=}d_{\mathrm{head}}/\sqrt{L}$ selects a non-geometric allocation; its $d_{\mathrm{head}}$ factor and $L^{-1/2}$ exponent are derived, and the remaining $\mathcal{O}(1)$ normalization sits inside an empirically flat PPL basin. Three primary stress tests anchor our evidence: in PE-dominant DAPE-style $128{\to}8\mathrm{K}$ extrapolation, EVQ improves over geometric RoPE and a learned positional operator; under matched YaRN scale on a $454$M passkey-mix model, EVQ+YaRN reaches $100\%$ passkey retrieval at $8\mathrm{K}$ while Geo+YaRN remains at $61\%$; and on a $432$M MLA model with $16$ frequency channels, EVQ alone reduces $2\times$ extrapolation PPL by $31.1\%$ at $+1.1\%$ in-distribution cost, surpassing Geo+YaRN. These results support training-time allocation \emph{shape}, not only frequency \emph{range}, as a first-order RoPE design axis.
\end{abstract}

\section{Broader Impact Statement}
\label{sec:broader-impact}

This work studies frequency allocation as a design axis for rotary position embedding rather than a specific deployed application. Its main positive impact is scientific and systems-oriented: replacing the default geometric frequency schedule with a principled closed-form allocation can reduce wasted compute and improve the reliability of long-context models across architectures. The main risk is over-generalization: our strongest evidence is concentrated on text extrapolation and retrieval-heavy long-context evaluation, so we do not present EVQ as a universal guarantee of downstream improvement across all tasks, modalities, or deployment settings.

\item \textbf{Claims}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Question:} Do the main claims made in the abstract and introduction accurately reflect the paper's contributions and scope?
\item \textbf{Answer:} \answerYes{}
\item \textbf{Justification:} The abstract and introduction are restricted to three contributions: (1) identifying training-time frequency allocation as a PE design axis with a closed-form variational family, (2) demonstrating that a semi-analytic rule---with derived $d_{\mathrm{head}}$ and $L^{-1/2}$ structure and an empirically validated $\mathcal{O}(1)$ scale---falls within the best observed sweep basin across the tested grid of 27 configurations (primary claims rely on 3-seed anchors; supporting 1--2-seed rows extend scope), and (3) the matched-scale systems result that EVQ increases YaRN leverage under a fixed YaRN scale (we do not claim dominance over every tuned YaRN baseline). The $\tau^*$ rule's epistemic status---derived $d_{\mathrm{head}}$ and $L^{-1/2}$ structure under the stated small-$\tau$ scalings, channel-load-motivated $\chi^2$ stiffness, and unit-convention $\lambda$---is stated explicitly in \S3.7.
